Chapter 14: Is This an Invasion?


The dedication of Carlos and Naomi, as they prayed for the guidance of the Holy Spirit, produced a growing transforma tion in the lives of the forgotten Uros. By the end of the first  school year, the children’s hair that had been dirty and matted was  combed. Their brown bodies were clean and free from lice. Thanks to the sewing of Naomi and help from missionary wives, every student wore a uniform to school. Quick young minds picked up the Spanish language as they learned to read and write.
Children who spent cold nights huddled with their families in tiny reed huts studied during the day in a floating school surrounded by the fantastic beauty of a twoand-a-half-mile-high lake. In Bible classes they learned of a Creator who made the sun, the moon, the stars, and love ly Lake Titicaca. The boys and  girls were polite and respectful. The songs they sang inspired them with hope.

After hundreds of years in miserable ignorance, Uro adults began  to glimpse a better future too. Carlos talked with the men. “You don’t  have birth certificates or other legal documents. You’re afraid to go

to the city for fear of being  detained by the police. Why  don’t you let me help you  solve this problem?” Many of the men liked  the idea, and Carlos made an  appointment to take them to  a military office. They arrived on the day indicated.  Carlos had them walk in  formation like soldiers. He  explained, “When the commander comes out, I will ask  you to stand at attention. I  will greet the officer, and he  will talk with you.” At the office, Carlos in formed the secretary, saying,  “The Uros are here.”
The commander came  out, and Carlos called, “Attention!” Immediately the men stood in formation. “Welcome to  Puno,” the officer said. “You’re a great looking group. You men will do  well serving in the army.”

Carlos translated this into Aymara. When they heard the words  “serve in the army,” the men disappeared. Not one stayed. They all  escaped. The commander laughed. He accompanied Carlos to the port.  None of the Uro men were there. Like animals fleeing from a predator,  they had vanished. “What shall I do?” Carlos asked. “I’ve tried and tried to get these  fearful people to apply for legal documents, and now they’re gone.” The kind officer understood. “Let’s go back to my office. I’ll give  you the necessary forms. You help the men fill in their personal infor mation. Then come by yourself and bring them to me.” Persuading the men to fill out the forms proved difficult. “The  commander will put us in the army,” the men objected. “Some of us  will be sold, and we’ll never see our families again.”

There was another obstacle—each applicant needed a medical cer tificate. Carlos worked with the Mission to find a solution. Dr. Sanchez  agreed to see the men and not charge anything for his services. The  examinations went well until a nurse began to draw blood. Suddenly  everyone believed the tales they’d heard about pishtacos were true.  “What will happen to us now?” they asked.
Carlos attempted to calm their fears. He explained, “They are not  drawing grease from your body. They are simply going to check a little  blood to make sure you are free from disease. Nothing will happen  to you. When I was in the army, they often drew my blood. It doesn’t  cause any problem.”
Carlos patiently worked with the men to complete their forms. He  even taught them how to sign their name. Carlos took the completed  forms and medical certificates back to the commander. As promised,  all who filled out the forms received their birth certificate and a legal  document making it possible for them to vote. With these documents,  the people could get marriage certificates. 
In the past, the Uros had secretly buried their dead at night. Now  they could get death certificates and permission to bury their dead  during the day, with no fear of the authorities. They had the privilege of

With these benefits, Uros who had lost con fidence in the teacher and the school began to have confidence again.  It took time, but more and more Uros applied for legal documents. No longer afraid, they visited stores, banks, and government offices.  When Carlos arrived at the floating islands, there was very little  tourism. Visitors who arrived on their own were usually chased off. A  French couple trying to visit a nearby floating island got into serious  trouble, and Carlos rescued them.
The head of the department of tourism for Peru visited the float ing island of Torani Pata in 1964. When he discovered Uros learning  Spanish, he decided Torani Pata with the Adventist school was the per fect place to bring tourists. Soon, people were coming from all over  the world, and tourism at Lake Titicaca has grown ever since. Carlos  encouraged the Uros to make souvenirs and sell them, instead of beg ging for money.

With help from ADRA, they built a large totora patio in front  of the school. Carlos’s students were finishing worship one morning

when a group of teachers and students arrived from the University of  San Augustine in Arequipa. They ran out to greet the visitors. The children all had a Bible and hymnal. “What are you doing?”  one professor asked.
“We were having worship,” the children responded. 
“What do you do in worship?” the professor wanted to know. “We sing and read our Bibles,” the children said.
“Read the Bible? What a waste of time!” the university professor  scoffed. “You are being deceived by religious fanatics.” At that moment, Carlos came out. His students introduced him to  the university group. Immediately a professor asked, “What’s going on  here? Your students say you teach the Bible.”
“Yes, I do. It’s a powerful book that transforms human lives and  helps people have a purpose in life.”
“That’s nonsense. At our university, we teach the doctrines of  Karl Marx.”
“Interesting,” Carlos said. “Why don’t you come into my school,  and we can talk about this more.”
Some thirty university visitors entered the floating school. Carlos  helped them find seats. “We are delighted to have honored guests from  a university,” he said. “Thank you for coming to visit our Seventh-day  Adventist floating school. We want you to feel welcome.”
A university professor stood and walked to the front. “Señor  Velasquez, the people you are trying to help will never make any prog ress with the Bible. It’s just a bunch of folklore passed down from one  generation to the next.”
“Wait a minute,” Carlos interrupted. “The Bible is the inspired  Word of God.”
The professor from the university continued. “You’re ignorant.  Unless you study Karl Marx, you don’t know anything. We want our stu dents to progress, so we teach socialism and the doctrines of Karl Marx.”
Walking toward a bookcase, Carlos smiled. “You say I’m ignorant,  because I haven’t studied Karl Marx.” He held up three volumes of Das  Kapital and the Communist Manifesto. “I want you to know, I’ve read  Karl Marx.”
He looked at the guest educator who had challenged him. “You  study Karl Marx. Have you studied the Bible?”
“No! Never!”

“I’ve studied both,” Carlos continued. “I find principles in the Bible  much superior to what Marx wrote. Please forgive me, but you’re igno rant if you haven’t studied the Bible.”
The group from the university remained silent, with no further  challenge.
Carlos spoke kindly. “Our mission has an office in Arequipa  located in Tingo. Why don’t you visit them and find out how you can  study the Bible?” As the group left, one professor remarked, “Something they’re  teaching in this school is making a difference. Did you notice how  clean and polite the students are?”
This is interesting, Carlos thought. The state church accused me of  deceiving the people. These university professors called me ignorant for  teaching the Bible. What will be the next challenge? By 1970, the military revolution that had begun in Peru two years  earlier was in control of the government, resulting in the erosion of de mocracy. The government took over private land, confiscated privately  owned haciendas, and nationalized oil companies. The Puno area of  Lake Titicaca became very much affected by the changes.
The military government, desiring to build up rural communities,  learned of successes in Puno and sent a large delegation to observe.  Hearing that poor Uro Indians living on floating islands were savages,  they made arrangements to visit and see for themselves. One day the steamship Ollanta, unable to navigate the shallow  water around the floating islands, dropped anchor out in the main bay.  People on the ship shouted, “Come and get us.” This is weird, Carlos  thought. This ship normally goes back and forth between Peru and  Bolivia. It has never stopped here before. The Uros went out to the ship  in balsas and began picking up passengers.
The visitors looked around and saw Escuela Flotante Adventista.  “Oh, no!” one man said, “these are missionaries. We hate people that  bring Yankee imperialism to our country. Why did Puno permit a  North American to come here?”
“We’re from the military. Where’s the gringo?” the men demanded. “We don’t know,” the Uros answered. “There’s no gringo on this  island.”
The Uros still had a certain fear of strangers. Hearing the word mili tary, they were stunned. Is this an invasion? Why did army people come in  civilian clothes? Do they plan to take over our island and make us slaves?

Balsas kept bringing more men from the ship—almost 200 of  them. No group this large had ever come to the Uros before. One  of the visitors pointed to Carlos, asking, “Is he the custodian?” No  one answered.
“Where’s the teacher?”
When Carlos stepped forward, they asked, “Are you the teacher?” “Yes, I am,” Carlos answered. “My wife and I have been working for  these people for several years now.
“Distinguished visitors,” he said, addressing the crowd. “Are you  tourists? What country are you from?”
“No! No! We’re Peruvians.”
“Wonderful!” Carlos exclaimed. “Welcome! Very few Peruvians  come here. Most of our visitors are from Europe or North America.  I’m sure you would like information about our work.”
“We’d like to see your school,” they responded. “We’re with the  military in Cusco, and we came to see how the revolution is going in  the Department [State] of Puno.”
“I’m from Puno,” Carlos said. “My parents lived in a rural area, but  they arranged for me to study at Colegio San Carlos. Later I served two  years in the army in Puno. I’ll be glad to share any information I can.  Since you want to see the school, let’s go inside to talk.”
As many as possible crowded into the classroom of the floating  school. Carlos began to speak.
“The Uros give you a big welcome. Before telling you about Puno,  I’d like to share what I know about campesinos in Cusco. I worked there  in construction from 1953 to 1956. I discovered the campesinos were  illiterate and ignorant. It made me sad to see practically no one knew  how to read or speak Spanish.
“You’re probably wondering why Puno is far ahead of Cusco in the  rural areas. I’m personally grateful for a missionary named Ferdinand  Stahl. He and his wife started schools for the Indians. The best thing  that ever happened to me is the years I attended Colegio Adventista del  Lago Titicaca near Juliaca. Puno is far ahead of Cusco because we have  Adventist schools in rural areas all around the lake. More recently, the  government has started schools too.”
The group listened in silence.
“Many of us in this area have been educated in Adventist schools.  We are loyal to our country and our God. Almost all in Puno know  how to read and write. Nearly everyone has finished primary school.
