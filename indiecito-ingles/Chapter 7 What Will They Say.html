Chapter 7: What Will They Say?


Carlos struggled with his thoughts. Why do I have to wait two days for an answer from Naomi’s parents? He had been out of  the army for a year, and he had just accepted a position as  principal of Colegio Fernando Stahl in Platería. It’s going to be a big  responsibility, he realized. Some of the teachers are married, and I would  like to be married by the end of the year.
Two days seemed like an eternity. Why didn’t Naomi’s parents make  an immediate decision like mine did?
Father, mother, and son arrived back at the grandmother’s home  on the evening of the second day. She immediately called Naomi’s fa ther and stepmother. The father faced the Velasquez family and spoke.  “We think it will be all right, but . . .” He hesitated. “What kind of wed ding will you have?”
Emilio thought for a moment before answering. “Your daughter  and our son are both Adventistas. Our son wants to have a Christian  wedding. There will be no dancing, no band, and no alcohol. After the  legal ceremony there will be a formal wedding in the church.”
Naomi’s father responded, “This is not what we want, but my mother  is an Adventista too, so it will be all right.” They discussed more details,  and both families agreed to go ahead with plans for the marriage.
Carlos, feeling excited about the prospect of getting married, ac companied his parents back home. “Thank you for letting God choose  for me,” he told them. “I know it’s His plan for me to marry Naomi. It’s  an answer to my prayer.”
As they walked, he thought about his family’s history. My grand parents refused to accept the gospel of Jesus, and they forced my parents  to marry. They didn’t learn to really love each other until they became  Christians. With God’s help, I will have a happy marriage from the start.
Carlos had never seen a Christian wedding, so he went to the  home of the Mission president in Puno. “Pastor Krause,” he began.

“You know I will be working in Platería. I don’t have any money right  now, but I will save a little each month, as I plan to be married at the  end of the school year. We want to have a church wedding. What do I  need to do? What kind of ceremony should we have?”
Pastor Krause asked, “Have you discussed the marriage with your  parents? This is the first step you should take.”
“Yes, our families have gotten together, and everyone has agreed  for us to marry.”
“Good,” the pastor said. “Another important point is your conduct.  Here in the Altiplano it’s the custom for couples to live together before  they are married. Bible-believing Christians must follow God’s Word  and refrain from relations before marriage. Only after the wedding will  the husband and wife sleep together.”
Carlos replied, “Yes, we want to keep ourselves pure and set  the right example for my students and our friends and neighbors.  I read the book The Adventist Home. God wants us to hold to the  highest standards.”
The pastor continued, “When you are ready to be married, you will  take your parents with you to the mayor’s office for a legal marriage.  But remember, in the sight of God you are not married yet. Next you  will go to the church. We will have a simple ceremony, reading texts  from the Bible, and you and your bride will publicly declare your love  for each other. We will then declare you husband and wife and pray for  God’s blessing on your home.”
“Is that all, pastor?” Carlos asked.
“You will probably want to have a reception, inviting all your  friends and neighbors. Remember, though, that you will not be serv ing alcohol and there will be no worldly music or dancing. It can be a  time of rejoicing, but not a time of frivolity. Jesus performed His first  miracle at a wedding feast. God will bless your wedding.” “Thank you, pastor. We will follow your counsel.”
The new school year began, and Carlos found that being princi pal of the school, besides teaching as well, kept him extremely busy.  Each month he put aside money to pay for the wedding. The ceremony  would take place when school was out in December. Naomi, wanting  to surprise Carlos, made her own wedding dress from white sheets. 
What originally seemed to be an eternity to Carlos turned out to  be like a moment. The day for the December wedding arrived, and  Carlos used some of the money he had saved to pay for a car to take his

bride and her family to the mayor in Acora. He paid the fees and soon  had the legal marriage document in his hands.
The hired car then took them directly to Platería, where a church  full of people waited.
Carlos, dressed in a black suit, stood with the pastor and watched  as his lovely bride, dressed in the white gown she had made from  sheets, walked down the aisle. The minister talked about God’s plan for  marriage. The couple knelt for prayer, and then they stood while the  pastor smiled and said, “In harmony with the laws of Peru and God’s  plan for marriage, I now pronounce you husband and wife. Ladies and  gentleman, I present Mr. and Mrs. Velasquez.”
The ceremony had been sacred and simple. Many who were see ing a church wedding for the first time said it was beautiful. Others  complained because there was no drinking or dancing, but many of the  young people said, “This is the kind of wedding we want to have when  we get married. We want our home to be blessed by God.” A reception  was held at the home of an Adventist church member in the pampa.
The newly married couple spent their honeymoon visiting  churches around the Platería Mission Station. Each day they gave Bible  studies to new people and visited families of church members to en courage them. Since school was out, the Mission, wanting to give the  couple a little financial help, hired Carlos to do this work during the  summer. The newlyweds worked together, visiting, praying, and study ing with families.
After three months of marriage, Carlos accepted an invita tion to teach at a mission school in Caijano Pata, where everyone  spoke Quechua. However, Naomi’s happiness at being the wife of an  Adventist teacher received a terrible blow the day they arrived at the  place of their new assignment. They had brought a bed and a few other  belongings. Like any good wife, Naomi asked, “Where are we going to  live?” There was no place for them to live. Finally a kind church mem ber who worked at home as a tailor offered them one small room with  a dirt floor. Together they set up the bed, and they used boxes to store  a few belongings.
The next morning Carlos met the school board chairman. “Sir, I  would like to see the school.”
“Before we look at the school,” he replied, “I want you to know  there is a government school about half a mile away. They’ve caused us  all kind of problems.”

“Really?”
“Yes, and the only way to get along is to fight them. It’s a one teacher school with a woman in charge.”
“Sir,” Carlos said, “I didn’t come here to fight our neighbors. I’m  here to help people. We need to learn to get along. I will do my best to  make friends. Let’s go visit this teacher. ”
“No way, Señor Velasquez. You can go by yourself if you want.” “I’m not afraid. I’ll go right now. The Bible says, ‘If it is pos sible, as much as depends on you, live peaceably with all men’ ”  [Romans 12:18].
Carlos left for the neighboring government school, wondering  what could possibly have happened to cause a fight. He had no idea of  the tension that existed between the Adventist group and the commu nity. Arriving at the government school, he introduced himself. “I’m  Carlos Velasquez.”
“And what are you doing here?” the teacher asked. 
“I’m the new teacher in Caijano Pata.”
“I’m not talking with you,” she said as she looked down. “Profesora! Listen! I’m not here to fight with anyone. We want to be  friends. I’d like to help you any way we can. We want to do whatever we  can for the good of the community.”
The woman’s attitude changed. “This is wonderful,” she said as a  smile crept across her face.
Carlos responded, “If there is any way I can help you, please let me  know.”
They shook hands, and Carlos hurried back to the school board  chairman. “Sir,” he said, “I’d like to see the school.” He called Naomi,  and they went together. Arriving at the site, they looked in amazement.  All they saw was four adobe walls and a dirt floor. No roof, no doors,  no windows, and no desks.
The board chairman explained. “I’m sorry. We don’t know what to  do. Enemies of the church burned our thatched-roof school.”  Carlos looked in disbelief. Naomi was perplexed. “Why did the  Mission send us here?” she asked.
“I don’t know,” Carlos responded. “God knows. I’m sure there are  boys and girls here who need a Christian education.”
Carlos contacted parents and began raising money. He bought  lumber, corrugated tin roofing, windows, and a door. Carlos and  Naomi worked on the school building with volunteers. Some of the

In just a few days, they were ready for classes to begin.  The one-room school opened with thirty children in grades one  to four. They studied subjects required by the government, plus Bible,  which Carlos made attractive to the children. He played with the stu dents at recess time. Having promised to be a friend of the teacher  at the government school, occasionally he went and played with the  children there.
School went well. Parents and students were happy. There was no  problem with the public school, and even the government education  inspector seemed pleased when he came to visit. The newlyweds used  up all their money, expecting to receive Carlos’s salary at the end of the  month. “Señor Velasquez,” the board chairman said, “we regret it very  much, but there is no money for your salary.”
He went on to explain. “The people here are very poor, and since  they gave everything they had to rebuild the burned-out school, noth ing is left to pay their children’s tuition.” 
Outrageous, Carlos thought. Unbelievable! We should pack up and  go home. We’ve spent everything we have. We don’t even have money to  get home.
Naomi saw their predicament. “The children like you, Carlos. We  came here to teach them about Jesus, and we can’t let them down. We  have no money, and there’s nothing to cook. I’m going out to work with  people in the harvest.”
For one day’s work Naomi received twenty-five pounds of pota toes. Without kerosene to use the primus they received as a wedding  gift, Naomi gathered sheep chips for fuel to boil potatoes. With nothing  else, they ate potatoes three times a day—potatoes and more potatoes.
At the end of first month—no salary. Second month—no salary.  Third month—no salary. Halfway through the school year someone  gave them five soles. Later someone gave them ten soles. Another  month passed, and they received fifteen soles. When they finally got  forty soles, they felt wealthy.
They learned to love these poor Quechua brothers and sisters, but  at the end of the year they sadly told them, “We won’t be able to come  back next year.”
Carlos pondered, Whoever dreamed I would not get my salary? My  wife is an amazing missionary. Instead of complaining, she went out and  worked in the fields to get us a little food. Without her, I would have

become totally discouraged. But now, what am I going to do? We have no  money to get home.
One church member took pity and offered to lend them a burro to  take their bed and other things as far as Juliaca. The family who shared  a room with them saw their desperate situation and gave them five  soles to take the train from Juliaca to Puno. Tickets for two cost three  soles. With the remaining two soles they rode on the back of a truck all  the way to their home in Ccota.
Reflecting on their first year of marriage, Carlos said, “You know,  Naomi, I never received a salary, we nearly starved to death, but thanks  to you, God saw us through. The potatoes you got for working saved  our lives. It’s time to think seriously about our future.”
“What do you think we should do, Carlos?” Naomi questioned. “We both need more education. I’ve prayed about it, and I think  we should go to Cusco. I can work in the daytime and study at night to  complete secondary. After our experience in rebuilding the school at  Caijano Pata, I’d like to learn practical things about construction. I’ve  noticed that few women around Lake Titicaca know how to sew. You  could study dressmaking.”
“Carlos, that’s something I’ve always wanted to do. I’d love to teach  women to sew. It would be a blessing for poor families.”
