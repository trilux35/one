Chapter 8: Worthless?


I’m sure glad Naomi likes the idea of going to Cusco, Carlos re flected. After a year of marriage, I know more than ever that she is the one God chose for me. Few women would put up with what  we’ve been through this past year. She’s agreed to go to Cusco, but how  can we get there? We have no money. And my parents can’t help.
Carlos explained his dilemma to Naomi. “Carlos,” she said. “I  still have four sheep on my grandmother’s farm. We could sell them  and use the money to buy train tickets.”
They were staying with his parents, who gave them plenty to  eat, but he realized it would not be good to continue to let them  provide for their needs. Confiding in his wife, he said, “I hate to  sell your sheep, but I see no other way to get to Cusco. We’ll have  to go on faith, because we don’t know the area and I don’t have a  job when we get there. All we have is a Bible promise, ‘He who  walks righteously . . . bread will be given him, his water will be sure’  [Isaiah 33:15, 16].”
Before going to the grandmother’s home to get the sheep, they  prayed. “Dear Lord, You know our situation. If it’s Your will for us to  go to Cusco, please help us sell the sheep.”
Will God answer our prayer? they wondered. Someone came  wanting to buy meat, so they butchered the animals. Another per son bought the wool, and a man came and paid a good price for the  hide. God has shown us His will, they concluded. “We have money to  buy the train tickets and some left over to buy food for our first few  days in Cusco,” Carlos said. Before doing anything else, they returned  tithe on the money earned from selling the sheep.
Hearing his daughter’s sheep were sold, Naomi’s father came to  see Carlos. “You’re nothing but a worthless bum,” he said. “I never  should have let Naomi marry you. You’re a total failure. How do you  think you’re going to live in Cusco? I despise you!”

The cutting words from his father-in-law hurt deeply. Carlos  thought, All I can do now is pray things will work out. What he says is  true. I haven’t finished secondary school, and I certainly haven’t provided  for Naomi during our first year of marriage. She’s the one who kept us  from starving, and her money will take us to Cusco.
They were getting ready to leave when someone said, “The Mission  plans to build a church in Cusco. It will be the first Adventist church  in the old Inca capital.” Realizing it might be difficult to get work in  Cusco, Carlos said, “This is something we had better check on.” They  went to the Mission and asked to see church administrators. 
“We’re moving to Cusco,” Carlos announced. “I hear you will be  building a church there. Will you need anyone to work in construction?” “We plan to start in three months,” Pastor Krause said. “I’m sure  some kind of work will be available. Why don’t you come and talk with  us when we start the project?”
Early the next morning, Carlos and Naomi purchased fourth-class  tickets and squeezed onto a hard wooden bench in the last railcar on  the train. Three bells rang. The whistle blew. The steam engine lurched  forward, pulling the train as they moved out of the station along the  edge of Lake Titicaca. By early afternoon, the train stopped at La Raya,  the continental divide at 14,172 feet elevation.
Carlos and Naomi got out and walked around. Looking east, they  saw the glacier on the Vilcanota mountains. “It looks like a huge dia mond reflecting the afternoon sun,” Naomi commented. “And what a  brilliant blue sky!”
Melting ice on the glacier formed a small stream that divided,  some of the water flowing to Lake Titicaca and the rest flowing all the  way to the Amazon and the Atlantic. Life is like this stream, Carlos  thought. Choices determine our destination. You never know where the  next turn will take you. 
The whistle blew, and they hurried back on board, reaching their  seats as the train slowly began to move. “It’s so beautiful here,” Carlos  said. “I’ve never been this close to a glacier before. But I’m feeling  sad, Naomi. I hated to leave Ccota with your father feeling the way  he does.”
“Don’t worry, Carlos. He’ll get over it. He’d be happy if you were  working as a policeman in Puno with a good salary, but I’m glad you’ve  chosen to be faithful to Jesus, regardless of the cost. We may have dif ficult times in Cusco, but it will all work out for the best.”

Carlos smiled. “Every day I thank God for a faithful wife like you.  I’ll never forget the day we met at the pastor’s gate in Platería. I knew  God was telling me you were the one.”
The train picked up speed. Dropping in elevation, they passed huge  alpaca and sheep haciendas with thousands of animals. The day grew  dark before the train finally pulled into Cusco. The trip from Puno  over the continental divide and down into Cusco had taken more than  ten hours. Immediately people began shoving and pushing as they  grabbed their things and scrambled off the train.
Each day, Carlos looked for work, but no job offers came. Soon  the little money left from the sale of the sheep was gone. Things could  not have looked more bleak. “Let’s sell the primus we got as a wedding  present,” Naomi suggested. “I’ll find paper in people’s trash and use it  to cook with.” The most anyone would pay was five soles, so they decided to keep the stove.
Carlos began working as a cargador, even though it was considered  the most demeaning work anyone could do. He met trains and carried  luggage for the more affluent passengers, who usually gave him fifty  centavos. With this he could buy five pancitos. Naomi laughed. “You  showed me where the Bible says our bread and water will be sure.”
One man offered Carlos carpentry work on condition he would  work on Sabbaths. “I’ll work any day except Saturday, but my con science doesn’t allow me to work on the Bible Sabbath,” he explained.
“Sorry, Señor Velasquez, I won’t be able to use you,” the man said. He found another job that had the same problem. “You’ll have to  work on Saturday,” the man insisted. Then Carlos interviewed with the  owner of a lumber yard. This man agreed to let Carlos work planing  lumber with a hand plane. On Friday afternoon, he received his pay  and rushed out to find his wife. “Naomi, here’s fifteen soles.” They hur ried to the market to buy a little food before sundown. During three months living on little more than bread and water,  Carlos lost so much weight that he looked like nothing more than a  pile of skin and bones. Pastor Krause arrived and announced, “We are  ready to start building the church. You can live at the construction site  so you won’t have to pay rent, and we’ll pay you six soles a day.” “Thank you, Pastor Krause. Every time I found work they insisted  I work on Sabbath, so I’ve had very little work.” Laughing, he contin ued, “I’m so weak from lack of food, I can hardly pick up a toothpick. I  can hardly wait to start really eating again.”

The builders threw away lots of scrap wood, and Naomi used these  to cook, which saved buying kerosene to cook with the primus. Carlos  took her to the dressmaker’s college, and she enrolled in classes. The  cost of thirty soles a month placed a heavy strain on their budget. “It  will be worth it in the future,” they decided. At the same time, Carlos  signed up to complete his secondary education at night school. 
The couple’s first son was born just before classes started the next  year. As the days passed, they woke the baby up at 3:00 a.m. so he  would sleep at the construction site while Carlos worked and Naomi  attended classes. This worked fine for two months, but then the baby  started sleeping less.
Carlos wondered, What can we do? I can’t take care of the baby and  work at the same time. They visited the dressmaking instructor. “We have  a baby and no place to leave him. Would you let the baby come to school?”
“You’re making a very unusual request,” the woman said and then  smiled. “Sure, I have a place where he can sleep. I think we can work  this out.”
Because of the teacher’s kindness, Naomi finished the two-year  course and received her dressmaking diploma. At the same time,  Carlos completed secondary school. By now he not only served as  caretaker of the church site but worked full time in construction,  served as paymaster, and took care of the accounts. After another year  of construction, the Cusco Adventist church was completed.
“God has been so good,” Carlos said to his wife one day. “We came  here with nothing. Now you have a diploma in dressmaking and are  ready to teach others how to sew. I’ve completed my secondary educa tion, which will be a big advantage in getting a teaching job. With the  practical experience I’ve gained in construction, I’m ready to tackle  most any building project. And praise God, we have a happy, healthy  boy.” In the back of his mind, Carlos had one concern. What will my  father-in-law say now? Does he still think I’m worthless?
With the Cusco church completed, the Mission president and trea surer came for the dedication. After reviewing the financial records  Carlos kept, Pastor Krause said, “We want you to stay another six months  while Pastor Amaro Peverini conducts a series of evangelistic meetings.” 
The treasurer, Brother Wilbur Olson, added, “Congratulations!  You are honest. In all the thousands of soles you’ve handled, we can  only find a loss of thirty centavos, which is nothing.” Pleased with  Carlos’s work, both men gave him a big abrazo.
