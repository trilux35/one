Chapter 5: Summer Meetings


Carlos’s hands shook as he knocked on the door of the princi pal’s office. A pleasant voice called, “Please come in.”
Pastor Jacobs offered Carlos a seat and then sat down be side him. “Carlos, we are happy to have you in our school. I understand  you and your friends have shown interest in your Bible classes and that  you are doing well.”
“Sir, that’s why I came here—I wanted to learn more about the  Bible.” In the back of his mind, he wondered, Why is it so urgent for me  to see the principal? I don’t think he called me in to say I’m doing well in  Bible classes.
Pastor Jacobs pulled his chair a little closer to Carlos. “At this  school you’ve discovered we are doing our best to follow the teachings  of Jesus as found in the Bible. I have a question: Is it true you’ve been  reading palms and telling students their fortunes?”
Carlos looked down at the floor. “Yes, it’s true. My parents prac ticed witchcraft and worshiped spirits. My father dedicated me to be come a witch doctor. I lived with my cousin while attending school in  Puno, and I had friends who were interested in the spirit world and  learned to do palm reading from them.”
Pastor Jacobs reached for his Bible. “The Word of God speaks  against this type of thing in many places. Let me read a couple of verses  in Deuteronomy 18. ‘Do not let your people practice fortune-telling, or  use sorcery, or interpret omens, or engage in witchcraft, or cast spells,  or function as mediums or psychics, or call forth the spirits of the dead.  Anyone who does these things is detestable to the Lord’ [vs. 10–12, NLT].” 
Pastor Jacobs leaned forward. “Carlos,” he said, “we want you in  our school, but if you ever engage in palm reading again, we will have  to ask you to leave. The Bible says fortune telling is an abomination.”
“I’m sorry,” Carlos said. “I know palm reading is from the devil. I  promise I will never do it again.”

“Thank you, Carlos. Let me pray with you.”
Back in the dorm, Carlos talked to his roommates. “I won’t be tell ing fortunes anymore. Pastor Jacobs showed me from the Bible that it’s  wrong. I know it’s the evil spirits that do it. I’ve decided I don’t want  anything more to do with the spirits. I want to forget everything I ever  knew about them.”
“One thing I like about this school,” Roberto said. “They seem to  have a reason from the Bible for everything they do.”
“Yes, that’s true,” Carlos agreed. “I like the verses in 2 Timothy that  we learned in Bible class the other day. ‘All Scripture is given by inspi ration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction,  for instruction in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete,  thoroughly equipped for every good work’ [verses 16, 17].” 
The school routine included study, work, and worship. The four  boys enjoyed their Bible classes. On Friday evenings they attended a  special class for new believers and learned even more. In a short time  they knew all the gospel songs by heart and could sing as well as the  other students.
Peru is south of the equator, where seasons are the opposite of  North America, so the school year ended in December. Roberto asked  to be baptized on the final Sabbath and decided to spend the summer  as a literature evangelist selling Christian books. Without making any  decision, Carlos and the other two boys went back to Ccota to work on  their parents’ farms.
On his first Sabbath morning at home, Carlos spoke to his father.  “I won’t be working today. It’s a holy day of rest. I want to take my sister  and worship in the church at Platería.”
“Why?” his father wanted to know.
“Papa,” Carlos explained. “Grandma Velasquez and you taught me  not to lie, not to steal, and not to be lazy. The Bible teaches this and  more. Listen! Here’s what the Bible says: ‘Remember the Sabbath day,  to keep it holy. Six days you shall labor and do all your work, but the  seventh day is the Sabbath of the Lord your God. In it you shall do no  work: For in six days the Lord made the heavens and the earth, the sea,  and all that is in them, and rested the seventh day. Therefore the Lord  blessed the Sabbath day and hallowed it’ [Exodus 20:8–11].
“Papa,” Carlos continued. “The Incas taught us not be lazy—God’s  Word agrees when it says, ‘Six days you shall labor and do all your  work.’ The Bible is clear about this. It says in 2 Thessalonians 3:10, ‘If

Carlos and his sister walked across the pampa. As they arrived  at the door of the church, Clara screamed, “No! No! No! I’m not  going in.”
What should I do? Carlos thought. My sister’s mind is full of all the  superstitions that used to scare me. She thinks something horrible will  happen if she goes inside an Adventist church. He took his sister by the  hand and walked out into the pampa. “Why don’t you just wait here,  and I’ll meet you when the services are over.”
Carlos talked with his friends in the afternoon. “Do you remem ber how it was when we first went to Chullunquiani? We didn’t know

“That’s a great idea!” Juan said. “But where can we hold meetings?” “Let’s talk to the Ccota school and ask,” Carlos suggested. During the week, the three young men approached the officials 
in charge of the local school. “We’ve been studying away from home,”  Carlos told them, “and we’d like to share the things we learned with  our families. We need a place to meet. Will you let us use the school?”
The man in charge saw the boys’ enthusiasm and said, “Since  school is not in session, it will be fine for you to meet in the school.  Just remember, you are responsible to keep it clean.”
Happy and relieved, the boys decided to conduct meetings  every morning at 5:00 a.m. and again in the evening at 6:30. “We’ll  take turns speaking and start on Saturday night,” Carlos suggested,  and his friends agreed. The three boys visited every home in the  area, inviting people to their meetings. But will people really come? they wondered.
The school room filled up for the very first meeting. People came  not only from Ccota, but from Palalla and other villages too. The boys  had prayer, songs, a Bible message—all in the Aymara language. After  the meeting, the boys prayed, “Thank you, God, for bringing so many  people to the school.”
The summer was filled with long days working on the farm and  meetings every night and morning. At the end of it, the boys headed  back to school. “I still can’t believe it,” Carlos said. “God blessed us. My  parents came the very first night, and they never missed a meeting. I  know it’s the work of the Holy Spirit.” 
Arriving back at Chullunquiani, they found Roberto unpacking  his belongings. “How did your summer go?” they asked.  “The Lord helped me,” he said. “I sold enough books to earn a  scholarship that will pay all my board, room, and tuition for another  year. I plan to sell Christian literature again next summer.” “We held meetings in a school,” the other boys said. “Even our par ents came, and they are beginning to understand why we are so happy  attending a Christian school. We plan to do this every summer.” What Carlos dreaded most happened at the end of the third year at  Colegio Adventista del Lago Titicaca. He had a year of secondary before

going to Chullunquiani, but since Peru has five years of high school,  he still had another year to finish before he could graduate. When he  and his friends turned eighteen, they were required to register for the  army. What will happen now? Carlos worried. Will I be able to finish my  studies and graduate?
Hoping for the best, Roberto went to the north end of Lake  Titicaca that summer to sell books, while Carlos, Emilio, and Juan went  home to help their parents and held more meetings. Pastor Sosa from  Platería lent them a battery-operated projector. This was their third  summer conducting meetings, and the pictures created new interest.  Two seniors from the Lake Titicaca Training School joined them and  helped organize a baptismal class.
A few days before that summer ended, Carlos and Juan received  official notices to report for induction into the army in two weeks. The  army, Carlos thought. This is what I feared most. How will I ever finish  my education?
Three summers of teaching practical Christianity to people in  Ccota and neighboring villages had been blessed more than the boys  could have imagined. One-hundred-twenty people, including Carlos’s  parents, requested baptism. Pastor Sosa came to baptize the people, ex
pecting Carlos to be among the candidates. “No, pastor, I’m not going  to be baptized,” he said.
His parents listened in disbelief. “Carlos, if you’re not baptized,  we won’t be baptized either,” Emilio said, looking directly at his son.  “What’s the matter?” he asked. “You taught us how to follow Jesus.  You showed us the evils of witchcraft and spirit worship. You helped  us decide to stop attending fiestas and to avoid chewing coca or  drinking alcohol. You showed us the joy found in obeying the Ten  Commandments. Are you going to turn against God?”
“Papa,” Carlos said. “You know I’m leaving for the army. What if  I’m too weak and fail to resist the temptations I’ll face? I don’t want to  make a promise to God and then break it.”
Carlos turned to Pastor Sosa. “I promise you right now, I’ll be bap tized as soon as I complete my two years of military service.” His parents were confused but decided to be baptized anyway.  Carlos was very happy to see his father and mother make their de cision to turn from the things of this world and follow Jesus Christ  the Savior. Yet in his own mind, he was troubled. What if something  happens to me before I complete my time in the army? John 3:5 is clear.
