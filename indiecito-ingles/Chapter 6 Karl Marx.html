Chapter 6: Karl Marx


After three years together at Chullunquiani, the four young men went different directions. Only Roberto stayed to  complete secondary school and continue working as a liter ature evangelist in the summer. Emilio accepted a position teaching  in an Adventist mission school. Juan was inducted into the army  at Juliaca.
On the day indicated in his papers, Carlos, with a sick feeling  in the pit of his stomach, checked in at the Army Induction Center  in Puno. An officer asked him to fill out forms, sign a pledge of loy alty to the government, and take an aptitude test. A captain ordered  him to stand in a long line with new recruits who were being issued  uniforms. What’s going to happen to me now? he wondered. I really  wanted to go back to Chullunquiani and finish school.
“Dear Lord,” he prayed. “You have led me in ways I never dreamed  possible. Please keep me faithful now.” He continued thinking. God  has guided me all along. My time in a Christian school changed my life.  I know the Holy Spirit was working when the priest showed us a Bible.  What if he hadn’t mentioned the Song of Songs? Where would I be if I  hadn’t bought a Bible? What would have happened if my parents hadn’t  changed their minds and let me go to Chullunquiani?
The long line where Carlos stood grew shorter. He would be the  next to get his uniform. A Bible verse he learned at school challenged  him. “You . . . must endure hardship as a good soldier of Jesus Christ”  (2 Timothy 2:3). Ordered to take off his civilian clothes and put on a  uniform, Carlos prayed, Dear Lord, please help me do my best to honor  the rules and respect my superiors. Give me courage to remain true to  the teachings of Jesus.
Officers took notice of a young soldier who acted very different.  “This army private reads his Bible every day,” they said. “He’s studious  and interested in learning.”

One officer commented, “If he’s reading the Bible, he’s read ing the wrong book. It will never make him a good soldier. We have  books by Karl Marx that are published in Russia and translated into  Spanish in Cuba. Let’s share these with him. His foolish religious ideas  will vanish.”
Not wanting to offend his superiors, Carlos began reading books  filled with revolutionary ideas, like the three volumes of Das Kapital and the Communist Manifesto. Communist philosophy is right, he  thought as the devil worked on his mind. Growing up as an Indian, I  saw members of my own family exploited by the wealthy. The way they  treat Aymaras is not right! Marx knows what he’s talking about.
Although this was ten years before Fidel Castro became the revo lutionary leader of Cuba, the ideas of this restless young Cuban and  his mentors, like Che Guevara from Argentina, were being exported to  poor nations in the Andes, including Peru and Bolivia. Carlos read the  books and struggled in his mind with the ideas of Marxism, but as he  looked for answers he also kept reading his Bible.
I should have known, he thought. God understands all the injustice.  Wicked oppressors will be judged. The Bible recognizes the problem by  calling attention to people who take advantage of others for their own  benefit. Bible verses almost leaped off the page as he read the book of  James. “Come now, you rich, weep and howl for your miseries that  are coming upon you! . . . The wages of the laborers who mowed your  fields, which you kept back by fraud, cry out; and the cries of the reapers have reached the ears of the Lord. . . . You have lived on the earth in  pleasure and luxury. . . . You have murdered the just; he does not resist  you” (5:1, 4–6).
Carlos saw that the Bible calls attention to the evil deeds of the  wicked and then says to those who have suffered at their hands, “Therefore  be patient, brethren, until the coming of the Lord” (James 5:7).
As Carlos read his Bible, he began to understand God’s solution to  social problems. Yes, there is injustice, he realized, but the world doesn’t  need a revolution. We need hearts and minds filled with love for others.  Jesus makes it very clear that He will take care of the problems of society.  He read what Matthew says concerning those who will be saved in  God’s kingdom:
The King will say to those on His right hand, “Come, you  blessed of My Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you

from the foundation of the world: for I was hungry and you  gave Me food; I was thirsty and you gave Me drink; I was a  stranger and you took Me in; I was naked and you clothed  Me; I was sick and you visited Me; I was in prison and you  came to Me.”

Or when did we see You sick, or in  prison, and come to You?” And the King will answer and  say to them, “Assuredly, I say to you, inasmuch as you did  it to one of the least of these My brethren, you did it to Me”  (Matthew 25:34–40).
Carlos weighed the issues. Karl Marx or Jesus? A verse he had  memorized at school popped into his mind. “Do not be overcome by  evil, but overcome evil with good” (Romans 12:21). Pastor Stahl came  to Peru to help us. He loved us. He and Mrs. Stahl took care of the sick.  They started schools to educate Indians in how to live better lives. They  brought the gospel and filled us with hope.
Why did my grandparents, Carlos wondered, reject the teachings of  the Bible taught by Pastor Stahl? Because of the schools he started, I’ve  learned a lot during three years at Chullunquiani. The officers promote  Marx. I’m going to promote Jesus. He’s the one with the real answers. I’ll  spend my life helping others. The best way to change the world is by doing  good.
Just a few days before completing two years of military service,  Carlos faced another temptation. His commanding officer gave him  a certificate that said, “We recommend Sargent Carlos Velasquez to  work for the police department.” The commanding officer said, “With  this certificate you will get a job and earn a good salary. You’ve been a  good soldier in the military, and they’re looking for men like you who  can speak Aymara, Quechua, and Spanish.”
A job with a steady income! Great! Carlos thought. That’s exactly  what I need as I go back into civilian life. But wait. I promised to be  baptized as soon as I got out of the army, he remembered. He learned  that if he took a job as a police officer, he would be required to work  on Sabbath.

Instead of applying for a police job when he left the army, Carlos  headed straight for his home in Ccota. “Listen,” he said when he saw  his parents. “My tour in the army is over. The officers tried to persuade  me to accept the principles of godless communism. They encouraged  me to take a job where I would work on Sabbath. But I remember  the promise I made. I’m determined to follow Jesus. The Bible says,  ‘Repent, and . . . be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ for the remis sion of sins; and you shall receive the gift of the Holy Spirit’ [Acts 2:38].”
The very next Sabbath, Carlos and twenty-five others stood with  the Mission president by the outdoor baptistry in Platería. With bright  sunshine filling a vivid blue sky, Carlos stepped forward and entered  the cold water. Pastor Oswaldo Krause raised his hand. “My dear  Brother Velasquez, because you have confessed all your sins, because  you have chosen to live for Jesus and prepare for a home in heaven, I  now baptize you in the name of the Father, the Son Jesus, and the Holy  Spirit. Amen!”
Emilio and Ignacia Velasquez rushed up to give their son a big  abrazo when he came out of the water. “Carlos,” his father said, “God  used you to give us the gospel of Jesus and hope for a home in heaven.  We thank Him that today you joined us as part of God’s family.” The  little church Carlos helped to organize in Ccota before leaving for the  army voted him in as a member.
A few weeks after his baptism, Carlos received a surprising mes sage. “The president of the Lake Titicaca Mission wants to see you in his  office.” The next morning Carlos left for Puno. “Hermano Velasquez,”  Pastor Krause greeted him. “The Mission Committee voted to ask you  to teach sixth grade at our Adventist school in Yanaoco. Will you ac cept the challenge?”
Without hesitation Carlos replied, “Yes, Pastor Krause. I want to be  a true soldier for Christ, so I’m ready to go wherever God sends me.” Teaching lively sixth graders proved difficult. Each day Carlos  prayed, “Lord, please help me to teach these boys and girls in a way  that will honor Jesus.”
He did his best, but at the end of the year there was no invitation  to teach another year. Why? he questioned. Am I a failure? Perhaps it’s  because I haven’t completed secondary school.
With no work for the Mission, Carlos went home to help his par ents on their farm. In the evenings he conducted classes for the Ccota  youth. One day after several months, Carlos sat eating with his parents.

His father looked across the small eucalyptus-wood table. “Carlos,  your mother and I have been talking. You are twenty-four years old.  You need to find a wife.”
Carlos listened as thoughts ran through his mind. I guess they don’t  know I’ve thought a lot about this. The Bible says, “He who finds a wife  finds a good thing, and obtains favor from the Lord” (Proverbs 18:22).  I bought books like Messages to Young People and What Every Young  Man Should Know. These books have excellent counsel. One says, “Young  men should allow the Lord to choose their life companion.” I believe this,  and I’m praying for divine guidance.”
His father continued. “You’re an adult now. You need to decide  who you want to marry so we can ask her parents to let her be your  wife. We think Señorita Suera would make an ideal mate for you.” “Señorita Suera?”
“Yes, she really likes you.”
Carlos appreciated his parents’ interest but kept thinking, I want  God to choose for me. I’ve talked to a lot of girls at church and in youth  meetings. I believe God will help me find the right one. His parents sug gested several other girls, but to each of them he said, “No.”
The Ccota church asked Carlos to serve as Sabbath School direc tor for the coming year. At the end of three months he went to Platería  to report the quarter’s activities. As he entered the gate going into the  pastor’s home, a house originally built by Pastor Stahl, an attractive  young woman came out. I’ve seen her at a distance, he thought, but  we’ve never met.
He spoke first. “Good morning, señorita, I’m Carlos.”  “I’m Naomi.” She smiled when Carlos shook her hand. “How are you?” he asked.
“Oh, I’m fine,” she responded. “I’m Sabbath School secretary for  the Platería church, and I just left my report with the pastor. And you?”  she asked, looking at Carlos.
“I’m Sabbath School director in Ccota. I’m coming to give my re port to the pastor.” Without further conversation they said good-bye. Naomi is really nice, Carlos thought as he knocked on the pastor’s  door. In seconds, the door opened, he greeted the pastor, handed him  his report, and turned to leave. On the way out he paused at the gate. I  was coming, and she was leaving. What’s going on inside of me? I’ve never  felt this way about any other woman. She seems like a real Christian. Did  God plan for us to meet here at the gate?

New thoughts rolled around in Carlos’s mind. What shall I do? If  I hurry, maybe she will still be around the plaza. He ran until he saw  Naomi. She’s not just nice, she’s beautiful. I love the way she braids her  hair. He approached and asked, “What are you doing here?”
“Oh, I just bought a few things and now I’m going home.” They  talked just long enough for Carlos to find out she lived in Huataraqui.  That’s near Lake Titicaca, he remembered. My parents own land in  Huataraqui. That will give me an excuse to visit her.
Carlos walked slowly toward home in Ccota. Naomi—what a beau tiful name. Naomi in the Bible was the mother-in-law of Ruth and a very  gracious woman. Naomi! Naomi! Naomi! He could not get Naomi out  of his mind. He’d been obsessed with the Song of Songs and the Bible.  He’d been obsessed with Chullunquiani. Smitten more than ever, he  became obsessed with Naomi.
Early one morning, Carlos told his parents he was going to  Huataraqui to check on the farm land they owned there. He really had  something else in mind. He found Naomi out working alone. They  greeted each other and began talking.
Finally Carlos looked into Naomi’s lovely brown eyes. “I want to  tell you what’s on my heart,” he said. “I just can’t forget you. Ever since  we met last Sunday I’ve been thinking about you day and night. You’re  the best Christian woman I’ve ever met.”
“This is strange,” she said shyly. “I’ve been thinking about you too.”  Carlos could hardly believe what he heard. He continued, “I’ve  been praying that God will guide me in finding the right person to  share my life. Do you think God is leading us?”
“I believe so,” she said. “But what shall we do?”
“Let’s get married,” Carlos blurted out. “I believe God has chosen  you for me.”
“Yes, let’s get married,” Naomi agreed.
Carlos’s heart beat faster as he told Naomi good-bye and turned to  walk home.
What shall I do now? he wondered. I’d better tell my parents since  they’ve been concerned about me.
That evening Carlos talked with his father and mother. “You’ve  been telling me I should get married, right?”
“Yes, you are surely old enough,” they said.
“I’ve been praying, and God has helped me find the right one.” “Who is she?” they wanted to know.

“It’s Señorita Naomi. I met her at the gate in front of the pastor’s  home in Platería. She lives in Huataraqui.”
“Oh, we know Naomi. She’s a fine young woman. Her mother died  when she was very young, and after her father remarried, she went to  live with her grandparents. They are faithful Adventistas. We need to  ask her family for permission for her to marry you.”
The next day Carlos raced back to Huataraqui and found Naomi  out working again. All out of breath, he said, “I want you to know I  will be coming back with my parents. They want to ask your family for  permission for you to marry me.”
“Please do come,” she said.
Later that day, Emilio, Ignacia, and Carlos trudged across the  pampa heading for the home of Naomi’s grandparents. “We’ve been  waiting for you,” the grandparents said. “Naomi told us you were com ing. What can we do for you?”
Emilio and Ignacia got right to the point. “Our son loves Naomi,  and we would like your permission for her to marry him.” “Interesting,” the grandfather chuckled. “I think we had better call  her father.”
Minutes later, he was back with Naomi’s father and stepmother.  They listened attentively to the Velasquez family’s request. Naomi’s father hesitated before responding. “We need time to  think about this. We will talk it over. Come back the day after tomor row, and we will let you know our decision.”
After thanking them, the Velasquez family got up and left. Greatly  troubled, Carlos questioned, What will I do if they say no? Why are  they making us wait so long for an answer? Do they think I’m not good  enough for their daughter?
