Chapter 12: The Floating School


Aboat is coming! A boat is coming!” Uros shouted as they ran to join Professor Velasquez. No one could see it, but they  heard the roar of a powerful engine. The captain of the port of  Puno had volunteered to use a navy patrol boat to tow the new float ing school to the island of Torani Pata, where children had grown up  for centuries without a school. The arrival of a floating school in April  1963 would change their lives forever.
The captain carefully maneuvered through the narrow channel  surrounded by totora reeds. The launch carrying Mission admin istrators and their families followed close behind. “I see it! I see it!”  Uro children screamed. Carlos directed the boat to the site where the  school would be anchored. The bright corrugated-metal roof gleamed  in the late morning sun.

Carlos was first to step inside the new building. Walking across  the rough wood floor, he exclaimed, “It’s nice to step on something  solid after walking on spongy totora for a month.” The missionaries  worked to get the school properly anchored before leaving. They left  a crew headed by Roberto Coila to install the metal siding, windows,  and doors. 
Outdoor classes continued while the crew worked to finish the  construction. When the work was completed, the mission launch re turned, bringing Carlos’s wife, Naomi, his three children, and their  few belongings. He helped his family out of the boat. Their new home  would be a small room on one end of the school. Husband and wife  worked together to set up a bed—their only piece of furniture. No  chairs, no table, no dresser, no drawers. The children slept on the floor.
The one-burner kerosene primus they had failed to sell in Cusco  served for cooking. To eat, they all sat in a circle on the floor. As a boy,  Carlos had grown up in a home where everyone ate with their hands.  We’re blessed, he thought. We each have a plate and a spoon. No one  complained that they didn’t have a fork or a knife.
The family struggled to get used to a new diet. Fish for break fast. Fish for dinner. Fish for supper. Naomi had brought a bag full of

The Lake Titicaca Mission, supported by local people and occa sional subsidies from church headquarters in the United States, found it  difficult to pay teachers what they deserved. This was especially true for  the floating school, where students paid no tuition. Carlos and Naomi  never complained. They always said, “God sent us here, and we will do  our best.”
The family’s clothing and books were stored in boxes. They did not  own a radio, and Carlos couldn’t even afford a watch. Curious Uros  became fascinated with their one luxury—a windup alarm clock. All  day long—tick, tick, tick. The Uros had never seen anything like it. They  would point and say, “It has a spirit.”
Witch doctors for centuries had taught fear of spirits to the Uros.  They used this fear to gain control over people’s lives. As the highest  authority, the witch doctor counseled with people about the most in timate details of life. The primitive Uros held no legal documents—no  birth certificate, no marriage license, no death certificate, and no docu ment to show they existed.
When young people wanted to marry, they went to the witch doc tor. This is abusive and disgusting, Carlos thought when he learned that  part of the marriage ceremony often involved the witch doctor dem onstrating sex to the bride while the groom watched. He would have  found many things hard to believe if he had not grown up in a home  where witchcraft was practiced. He knew firsthand that the devil is be hind much of what goes on.
The old blind witch doctor, Emilio Porcela, had not shown himself,  even though Carlos kept hearing about him. He lived on another is land but served as leader for all the floating islands. After the arrival of  the school, the witch doctor became concerned, perhaps thinking, This  teacher with a building will steal my influence.
After his students left for home one afternoon, Carlos found Emilio  Porcela waiting on the island outside the school. “Why are you here?”  the witch doctor demanded. “You’ve come to earn lots of money!”  he declared.
If only he knew the truth, Carlos thought. It’s really tough to support  a family with children on the equivalent of $15.00 a month I receive from

This is what I want to do for the Uros.”

“True,” Carlos said. “I served as principal of Colegio Fernando  Stahl for one year. Stahl is a good name, but I want you to know I  like your name—Emilio Porcela. My father’s name is Emilio. You may  not remember, but I met you when you came to Puno. You visited the  home of the owner of the room where my cousin and I stayed while  attending school in Puno. You came at night.

“I was curious because my parents practiced witchcraft and want ed me to become a witch doctor. You contacted the spirits, asking them  to bless a man who was starting a new business.”
“Oh, oh, yes,” the witch doctor said. “That was many years ago. The  man paid a large sum of money, and I held the ceremony at night for  fear of getting into trouble with the authorities.”
“Yes,” Carlos agreed. “It was a long time ago. I was just a young boy  and never dreamed I’d ever see you again. I’m glad you are here, and I  want to thank you for telling your people not to harm me. My wife has  our evening meal ready. Why don’t you come inside and eat with me?”
They sat on the floor around the big kettle. Naomi handed their  guest a plate and a spoon. Carlos explained, “We believe in the God  of heaven. He made us. He made Lake Titicaca. He made everything.  Before we eat, we like to thank Him.” Carlos and his family bowed their  heads for grace.
“Father in heaven,” Carlos prayed. “We thank You for all You do for  us. Please bless our food and give us health and strength to serve You. In  Jesus’ name, amen.” While they were eating together, the witch doctor 
learned more about Carlos and the school. A few days later his grand son, a bright boy named Juan Santos Porcela, enrolled in the school.  Juan asked many questions. Bible stories especially fascinated him.

More new students came to the school, yet many parents con tinued to spread false rumors. Some of the illiterate Uros seemed  more concerned with gringos and pishtacos than taking advantage of  the opportunity to educate their children. “The teacher is a gringo,”  they insisted. “Just wait! He seems kind, and children like him, but  our children will suddenly vanish. Señor Velasquez will get rich sell ing grease from our children’s bodies. We must stop him before it’s  too late.”
Carlos shared his concerns with Naomi. “I know they didn’t want  a school, but calling us gringos makes no sense,” he said. “I’ve got to  convince them that we are one of them. The Uros have intermarried  with Quechuas and Aymaras. Why can’t they accept us for what we  really are? The children in Puno were right when they called me a ‘little  Indian.’ I’m an Indian just like them.”
Naomi suggested, “Perhaps the Uros need to meet your parents.”  “That’s a wonderful idea!” Carlos agreed.
He rented a sailboat, and with about thirty students and twenty in terested adults, sailed across the blue waters of Lake Titicaca’s Chucuito  Bay. Leaving the boat at the edge of the lake, they walked along a rocky  trail to Ccota. Carlos presented his mother, Ignacia. She smiled and  declared, “This is my son.” He introduced his father. Emilio greeted the  guests and, turning back to Carlos, said, “This is my son.”
After introducing Carlos’s brothers and sister, the Velasquez fam ily served food to their Uro guests. Later that afternoon, as the Uros  sailed back to Torani Pata, they talked about what they had seen. “We  thought he painted his skin to deceive us, but he looks just like his  family. He can’t be a pishtaco.” No one ever called Carlos gringo again.
Although the people began to show more confidence, it would  take years for the Uros to believe they were doing the right thing in let ting a teacher from the outside bring a floating school to their village.  Parents approached Carlos with questions. “Why are you teaching our  children not to drink alcohol or chew coca?”
“Alcohol and coca harm the body,” he replied. “We want you to live  long, happy lives.”
“But, Professor Velasquez,” they argued. “We live on water, not  land. To spend your life living on water you must use alcohol. Without  it you will die. Coca is necessary too!”
At harvest time, the Uros invited Carlos and his family to join  them in a trip to the big island of Amantaní. After beginning the trip

in a large boat, they all started drinking. They handed a glass of alcohol  to Carlos. “Thank you,” he said. “I don’t drink.”
“If you don’t drink, you’ll get sick. You’ve got to drink a little glass full, at least.”
Carlos stood firm. “I’m not going to drink!”
“If you don’t drink, you and your wife will get very sick.” “All right,” Carlos countered. “If I get sick, if my wife gets sick, if  my children get sick, then you have a point. Let’s see how my family  does without alcohol during this trip.”
To reach the big island of Amantaní, they sailed all night. By  morning the Uros were all sick, but Carlos, his wife, and children felt  fine. No one in his family got sick. “Listen,” he said to the Uros, “ev erything you’ve said about the benefits of alcohol and coca has proved  false. You drank alcohol and chewed coca, and you’re all sick. My fam ily avoided alcohol and coca, and we’re all fine. We have learned to  take the best possible care of our bodies and trust in God. He takes  care of us.”
Every night at the school where they lived, Carlos lit a kerosene  lamp. The soft light flowed out through the windows. The islanders  imagined, The teacher is praying to protect his family from evil spirits. They believed the mountain has spirits and the lake has spirits. Spirits  could punish and cause a person to die. The people were afraid of the  spirit in the teacher’s alarm clock.
With life centering around witchcraft, the people kept a balsa as  a spirit-shrine anchored near the entrance to their island. To appease  the spirits, they worshiped the lake and made offerings by pouring the  blood of birds into the water.
Discussing this with Naomi, Carlos commented, “The Uros are ig norant, fearful, and superstitious in many ways, but in other respects  they are very intelligent. They know the lake like no one else. With the  right education, they will live successful lives.”
“One thing is sure,” Naomi replied. “They make good use of what  they have.”
Primitive though the people were, the teacher and his wife found  the floating island people remarkable. The men fished, cut totora, and  built balsas. And not just one balsa. Each family had at least three.  While one was for fishing, a larger balsa was used mainly by the women  when they took products to the mainland to be sold or bartered. The  larger balsas were also used for trips to the big islands like Taquili and

Amantaní. A small balsa served best to go through  the reeds hunting bird  eggs.

Both men and women  spent a lot of time cutting  totora. They had to be con stantly adding new layers  to their island as the bot tom rotted away. A balsa boat used daily may not last  longer than a month, while  one with little use might  last nearly a year. A good  reed hut would last almost  a year, but before the rainy  season the men always  added totora to the roof of  their huts.
Women searched for  dried totora to use as fuel  for cooking. They spent a 

lot of time cutting and drying fish. Two or three days before a fies ta, they would bake fish. Selling fresh, dried, and baked fish provid ed the main source of family income. Routine work kept the women  constantly occupied.
Carlos mentioned to his wife that he had been watching the  men. “They often leave at two or three in the morning to fish,” he  said. “They seem to know exactly when and where to go. Only the  Uros know why, but when there are bright moonlit nights they fish in  the daytime.”
“And the women,” Naomi said. “They don’t have calendars, but  they know exactly when the fiestas are held. They always take their  children in a balsa and go to Puno or Chucuito to sell their products.  And they always remember to buy alcohol, coca leaves, sugar, and if  they have money left over they buy bread too.”
Carlos and Naomi noticed that the men never went to fiestas in the  city for fear of being picked up by the authorities for lack of legal docu ments. Yet they often ventured to fiestas held in rural areas.

One young man came back from a fiesta with a little transistor  radio. After a few weeks he came to Carlos at the school. Holding up  his radio, he said, “It won’t talk anymore. Please fix it for me.”
What can I do? Carlos wondered. Since I’ve never owned a radio, I  don’t know anything about fixing one.
“What do you do to make it play?” he asked.
“Look,” the boy said, pointing to a switch. “This is how I turn it on  and off. When I want to find a station I turn this little knob.” “Does your radio have batteries?” Carlos questioned.
“Yes.”
“Please show me how you take them out.” After the boy took the  batteries out, Carlos asked him to put them back in again. Not under standing the positive and negative ends of the batteries, the boy had  put them in backwards. “Let me see the radio,” Carlos requested. He took the batteries out  and turned them around so that they had the proper connections for positive and negative.
He handed the radio back to the boy, saying, “Please turn it on.” The boy turned it on, and it began to talk. “Thank you! Thank you!” the boy said with a big smile. He went back to his hut believing the teacher could fix anything. Both children and adults were fascinated by the boy’s 
radio. “How does it work?” they kept asking Carlos. He didn’t know how it worked, but he planned a way to help the people understand the mystery of radio. Carlos arranged for  the school children to

visit a radio station. “Be sure and keep the radio on all the time we are  gone,” he told the owner of the little radio. At the station, the program  director had the children sing and then greet their families in Aymara.
When they arrived back at their island, they were welcomed by  excited family members. “We heard you! We heard you on the radio!”  they shouted. “Your voices talked on the radio.” The children tried to  explain how they spoke into microphones.
This caught peoples’ attention, giving Carlos the opportunity to  teach the parents that there is a God in heaven who hears us when we  pray. “You could hear your children’s voices, even though they were in  Puno,” he said. “In the same way, God is able to hear us when we talk  to Him in prayer. We don’t need to consult with evil spirits.”
While Carlos taught the Uro families many new things, his own  family had much to learn. His children complained: “Why did you  bring us to live in such a horrible place? There’s nowhere to play except  on these spongy reeds or in the balsas. About all you feed us is fish.  We’re tired of fish. Every time we need to go to the bathroom, we have  to borrow a balsa boat and go about 600 feet back into the totora reeds.”
Playing with the other children, they learned to handle balsas well.  Looking out the window one day, Carlos saw his oldest son, four-year old Yban, fall off a balsa into the lake.
Fear gripped his heart. My son can’t swim and will drown. Horrified,  he watched the boy disappear beneath the surface of the water.

Carlos ran out and shouted to children who were playing in a  balsa. They managed to pull the boy out of the water with a long pole.  Naomi had heard the commotion and reached her husband’s side by  the time the children with the balsa reached the edge of the island.  They looked at the Yban’s lifeless form. His little body had turned red. 
Having had a first-aid course, Carlos immediately began mouth to-mouth resuscitation on his little son. Naomi watched, and, seeing  no reaction, she cried, “Oh, he’s dead.” Desperate to know what to do,  she knelt by Carlos, who was already on his knees holding the boy.
They stopped a moment to pray, and Carlos did more mouth-to mouth resuscitation. The boy coughed. Water poured out of his mouth  when his father turned him over. A little more resuscitation, and the  boy opened his eyes and began to breathe normally.
Uros who watched the husband and wife pray began thinking,  Maybe there is a God in heaven who answers prayer. Still holding the  boy, Carlos hugged his wife. “God is so good,” he said. At the time  they could not have imagined this boy would grow up and study medi
cine. He moved to the United States and had his children educated at  Andrews University.
After almost losing his son, Carlos hired a man to build him a  balsa. More important, he taught his children and the school children  to swim. 
The Uros drank contaminated water taken out of the lake around  their homes, and almost all of them suffered with parasites. Carlos and  his family went out in the lake to get water where it was cleaner, and  boiled it before drinking. Whenever possible, they also brought water  from a spring in Chimo.
A fifty-five gallon drum cut in half served as a bathtub. They filled  it early in the day, let the water stand in the sun until bath time, and  each family member took a turn getting a lukewarm bath. Carlos soon  discovered that urging Uros who never had a bath in their life to take  baths brought no results. Finally he invited them to a demonstration.  The people laughed at first, but the idea began to catch on, and many  began bathing.
All the children suffered with fleas and lice, so Carlos went to the  government health center and asked for help. The workers there gave  him half a sack of a powerful insecticide and equipment for spraying  it on. Knowing the Uros would be afraid to use it, he sprayed his own  clothes, his living quarters, and the school, while the people watched.

He asked his students,  “How many would like to  be free from fleas and 

lice?” They began shout ing, “Me, me, me!” After he  treated each one, they asked  if they could have their reed  homes sprayed too. Some  parents were not sure, but  in the end they agreed. The next day the children an nounced, “Professor, we are  free from fleas.”
God is so good, Carlos  thought. I’m sure He guided  us to do this. The program  proved so successful, he visited other floating islands and helped the people free their homes and  bodies of insect pests too.

“Have you noticed the people here get up grumpy and often com plain of headaches?” Carlos asked his wife.
“Yes, and I think I know why. It’s their poor health habits.” “What do you mean?”
“Well, they hardly eat any breakfast, they eat very little at midday,  and then they eat a huge meal at night.”
Husband and wife began encouraging the people to eat a good  healthy breakfast, a good meal at noon, and much less in the evening.  Those who tried it found it beneficial. Carlos and Naomi realized, It  won’t be easy to change habits developed over the centuries and deeply  ingrained in the culture. We need patience. Constantly watched, they  did their best to set a good example.
“Naomi,” Carlos said one morning. “It seems like these supersti tious Uros are beginning to have confidence in the school.” Later that  morning the bright sun began to grow dark. Strange, Carlos thought.  Not sure at first what was happening, he watched shadows creep across  the lake as the sky darkened. Sun-worshiping Uros became hysteri cal when the sun disappeared, leaving only the white corona. They  pounced on Carlos. “It’s your fault. You’re a devil,” they shouted. “We  will die. We should have never let you bring a school to our islands.”

As the sun slowly began to reappear, Carlos explained. “We’ve just  experienced a total solar eclipse. This happens when the moon passes  between the earth and the sun. In a few minutes, the sun will shine as  bright as ever.” Anger turned to hope, and the people marveled at the  knowledge of the teacher.
