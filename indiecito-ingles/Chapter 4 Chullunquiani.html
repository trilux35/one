Chapter 4: Chullunquiani


Carlos fell asleep thinking about life without interference from his parents. At nearly midnight, he awoke abruptly.  “Carlos! Carlos!” He opened his eyes. Papa and Mama were  standing by the bed, his father holding a candle. “You are still here?”  Papa said. 
“Yes, but I’m about to get up and leave. I have everything ready.” “Listen, Carlos,” his father said. “Your mother and I have been  thinking about your future. Even though we are not Adventistas, let’s  talk about Chullunquiani with the pastor in Platería. We’d hate to see  you all alone in a big city like Arequipa.”
Carlos thought, I can learn to sing.
At dawn, he stood with his father and mother on the doorstep of  Pastor Sosa’s home at the Platería Mission Station. Emilio thought to  himself, Are these Protestants really devils like we’ve always heard? He  breathed deeply and knocked.
The door opened, and Pastor Sosa smiled. “Good morning!  Welcome! Why have you come so early? Please come in, Don Emilio  and Doña Ignacia. I’ve heard about you. Is this your son?” “Yes,” they responded.
“Please sit down,” the pastor said.
Why were we so afraid? Emilio wondered. In Acora or Puno, they  always have us sit on the ground with the other Indians. We’ve never  been treated so well.
“Don Emilio,” Pastor Sosa spoke. “Why are you here? What can we  do for you? This is the first time you’ve come to visit me.” “Pastor, this son of ours wants to study at Chullunquiani. Like  us, he doesn’t know how to sing, he doesn’t understand the Bible, and  he doesn’t know how to pray. Would it be possible for him to go to  Chullunquiani? Would they accept him as a student? He completed the  first year of secondary at Colegio San Carlos in Puno.”

“Wonderful!” the pastor said, smiling at his visitors. “There will  not be a problem. They will teach him wonderful lessons found in the  Bible. He will learn to sing gospel songs. He will get a good education.”
Immediately Carlos’s father asked, “How much will it cost?” “It’s very reasonable,” Pastor Sosa replied. “It’s only two hundred  soles for the entire year, including room and board. As part of a practi cal education, Carlos will be required to work.”
Emilio reached into his pocket. “I brought fifty soles. May I give  you fifty soles in advance?”
The pastor accepted the money and wrote out a receipt. Turning  to the parents again, he said, “Your son is registered. Be sure he gets to  Chullunquiani before the end of March.”
Carlos and his parents trudged across the pampa heading home.  Heavy rains the day before had flooded the area, and they walked care fully. Carlos could hardly believe the change in his parents. Last night  my father said “Never,” and now he’s made a deposit for me to study at  Colegio Adventista del Lago Titicaca. 
They were almost home when Carlos saw a cousin who had the  same name as his father, Emilio Velasquez. He left his parents and ran  ahead. Reaching his cousin’s home, he called, “Hey, listen, I’m going to  Chullunquiani.”
“Really?” Emilio exclaimed. “I want to go too! Let’s go see Roberto  who lives on up the hill.”
“Sure, let’s go,” Carlos agreed. 
The two cousins were out of breath when they reached Roberto’s  home. Roberto came running out when the boys called. “Look,” they  said, “both of us are going to Chullunquiani.”
“Great,” Roberto congratulated them. “I want to go too!”  “Now there are three of us going to Chullunquiani,” Emilio said.  “Do you know Juan Flores who lives at the bottom of the hill? Why  don’t we talk to him?”
“Let’s go!” Racing down the hill together, they saw Juan out walk ing and shouted, “Juan! Hold up! We want to talk to you.” “Listen,” they said when Juan reached them on the steep hillside.  “We’ve all studied in Puno. We wanted you to know that the three of us  are going to Chullunquiani.”
“Me too! I want to go,” Juan said. “When will we go?”
Carlos spoke up. “The pastor at Platería says we have to be there  before the end of the month.”

“If that’s the case, we’d better talk with our parents right away.” On that day, four boys who grew up in superstitious, spirit worshiping families got permission from their parents to go to the  Adventist school. Another spirit was working—the Holy Spirit. By the end of March, the boys were ready with their bedding,  clothing, and a very few personal belongings. The four looked for a  truck to take them to Puno. With no trucks that morning, they decided  to walk. After spending a night with friends in Puno, the boys found a  truck headed for Juliaca. They climbed in with their bundles, sat hud dled together on the floor of the truck bed, and pulled their ponchos  tightly around them to keep warm.
The truck bounced along on a rough dirt road, stopping occa sionally to pick up other passengers who crowded in. One passenger  warned the boys, “When you get to Juliaca, you will have to walk three  miles to reach the school at Chullunquiani.”
After a long, dusty ride on a washboard road, the truck stopped  near the train station in Juliaca. The boys climbed out, asked for di rections to Chullunquiani, and started walking. The driver of a pick up stopped right beside them. “Hey, boys,” he called. “Where are  you going?”
“We’re on our way to Chullunquiani.”
“That’s where I’m going,” the driver said. “Jump in the back and I’ll  take you there.”
The driver took off as soon as they had settled in the back of his  pickup. Suddenly, fear filled their minds as they bumped along in a  pickup driven by a total stranger. None of them spoke. How do we  know he’s going to the school? Carlos thought. Maybe he’ll haul us off to  some big hacienda and make us work as slaves.
Their fears vanished when the boys saw a big sign saying, “Colegio  Adventista del Lago Titicaca.” The pickup slowed down and drove  through a gate in an adobe wall surrounding the school. The boys un loaded their bundles. Carlos looked around. This is different. I thought  it would be like San Carlos in Puno. I don’t see any school uniforms.  “Look,” he said to the other boys. “The girls are all dressed in polleras.”
“Yeah,” they replied. “Everyone’s dressed like Indians, and they’re  all out working. What kind of a place is this? Will we be slaves?” The pickup drove away, and several students surrounded the boys.  “Welcome! Where are you from?”
“We’re from Platería,” the boys answered.

“We’re from Platería too, but we’ve never seen you.”
“We’re really from Ccota. You wouldn’t know us because we’ve  never gone to the church in Platería.”
The school principal, Pastor Jacobs, came out and greeted the boys.  “I see you all have bundles with your belongings. You must be planning  to attend school here.”
“Yes,” Carlos said. “I sent fifty soles with Pastor Sosa as the initial  payment.”
The other boys said, “We have our money and are ready to pay.” After taking care of the financial arrangements, the principal led  the boys to their room in the dormitory. “We are putting you in room  number seven,” he said.
The boys looked around. There were no beds, but in each corner  of the room they saw adobe bricks stacked up about eighteen inches  high, forming a place to sleep about the size of a camp cot. Like Carlos,  all the boys were used to sleeping on a dirt floor, so this was not a  problem.
By mid-afternoon they were settled. Juan had brought a soccer  ball. “Let’s go play,” he suggested. 
They were having a great time when a teacher called, “What are  you doing?”
“Oh, we’re just playing.”
“I’m sorry,” the teacher said. “We don’t play soccer here.” The four young men frowned, picked up their ball, and walked  back toward the dormitory. “This is disgusting. How can you have a  colegio without soccer?” Juan muttered. “Everything here is strange.” Carlos remained silent. I talked these friends into coming to this  school. What have we gotten ourselves into? Finally Carlos suggested,  “Let’s go talk with the principal and ask for permission to play.” Four unhappy young men trooped into Pastor Jacobs’s office. “Sir,  the four of us come from a Catholic school in Puno. Please give us  permission to play soccer.”
The school principal stood thinking for a long time. Not realizing  a teacher had already told the boys they couldn’t play, he finally said,  “All right, you can play for a while provided you stay right here in front  of my office.”
“Thank you, sir, thank you very much.”
The boys began kicking their ball around, and other students came  out to watch. “Come join us,” they called.

“No,” they answered. “It’s prohibited. This school promotes physi cal labor for exercise. They teach us that competitive sports often create  unhealthy attitudes. Have you heard how over 200 people were tram pled to death in a stadium during a soccer game in Lima?” 
The boys grumbled but kept on playing. How can a school like this  exist? You can’t even play football. This is the most absurd thing we’ve  ever heard.
At exactly 5:30 p.m. the bell rang. All the students headed to  a building across the campus. “Come!” a student called. “It’s time  to eat.”
The boys put away their soccer ball and followed the students  headed for the dining room. I’m really hungry, Carlos thought. I’ll ask  for churrasco and black coffee.
Carlos reached the serving deck ahead of his companions. “Señora,  please give me churrasco with strong black coffee.”
A smile broke across the woman’s face. Trying not to laugh, she  stepped up close. “I see you’re a brand new student,” she said. “We don’t  serve churrasco here. We want our students to have the best possible  diet, so we only serve vegetarian food.”
“And what about coffee?” Carlos asked.
“We don’t serve that either. We never give our students any drink  containing caffeine.”
The four new students eyed each other. What will they feed us? All  the students stared at them. They were served boiled potatoes, soup,  salad, bread, and herb tea.
“I don’t like this. It’s awful,” Carlos blurted. “Since there’s nothing  else, we’ll have to eat it.”
After supper on Friday evening, the bell rang again and students  began walking to the chapel, where they had assigned seats. For the  first time since arriving at the school, the boys were separated. Carlos  looked around. All the students were dressed in their best clothes and  carried Bibles.
I should run back and get my Bible—the one I bought at the Sunday  market, he thought. Realizing the meeting was starting, he decided to  stay where he was. Looking around, he noticed that not only did every one have a Bible, they all had hymnals too.
Carlos was embarrassed to be in everyday clothes when everyone  was dressed up. He asked himself, Do I really want to learn to sing? He  listened. These students certainly sing with some gusto.

Suddenly all the students were on their knees. What are they doing  now? Carlos wondered. He looked for his friends. They were kneeling  like everyone else. Oh, no! I’m the only one standing. I’d better kneel too. 
Everyone had their eyes closed, so he closed his too. Before he real ized it, everyone was back in their seats. This is terrible. I’m the only one  kneeling and they’re all looking at me. I just keep doing the wrong thing.  Why did I come to this school?
Back in the dormitory, the Adventist students told them the pro gram they had just attended was called vespers. “We do this every  Friday at sundown to welcome God’s holy Sabbath. The Bible teaches  us to honor Jesus and keep the seventh-day Sabbath from sundown  to sundown. It starts at sunset Friday and ends when the sun goes  down Saturday.”
After breakfast the next morning, the boys attended Sabbath  School, where they sang and studied from the Bible. Next came a ser mon by a pastor who challenged them to be faithful, true, and honest.
After lunch the boys joined a group of students and a teacher for  a walk up the hill behind the school. Having grown up in the country,  Carlos loved the things of nature. The teacher explained how a loving  God made all the beauty of nature for us to enjoy.
Later in the afternoon Carlos and his friends went to a meeting  where young people led out in the program. They were impressed  when many students repeated Bible verses they had learned during  the week. 
Over the weekend, the boys got to know some of the other stu dents from Platería. They said, “We know everything is new and dif ferent for you, but we will help you all we can.” The pastor and teachers  took an interest in the four boys, encouraging them and helping them  to adapt. By Sunday morning, when they got their work assignments,  they were beginning to feel much more at home.
Roberto and Juan were sent to work on the farm. Carlos and his  cousin Emilio were assigned to work in the kitchen. Carlos mused,  There’s one thing we won’t be doing. We won’t be preparing churrasco.  What kind of food will we prepare?
While working in the kitchen, the girls often sang, and they asked  Carlos to join them in singing hymns. Soon he learned the words and  stayed on tune. This is great, Carlos thought. Ever since the priest told us  about the Bible and the Song of Songs, I wanted to learn to sing so I could  serenade the girls. Now these girls are teaching me to sing.

On Monday, Carlos and his friends attended their first classes.  That evening Carlos told his roommates, “My Bible class is really inter esting. I’m learning things I never heard before.”
Another boy said, “I’m amazed that every teacher, whether it’s in  science, or math, or language—they all pray at the beginning of class.” The four boys who, at the beginning, had found everything strange  and different began to settle down and enjoy their work and study.  Carlos would be the first to get into trouble.
Having grown up in a family that practiced witchcraft, Carlos had,  while living with his cousin in Puno, become interested in the occult  and learned to read palms. One day at work, he mentioned this to a girl  who, like him, came from a home that knew nothing about the Bible.  Immediately she wanted her palm read.
“All you will have to do is tell me the exact date of your birth. After  that I will look at the lines in the palms of your hands and tell your  fortune.” Word circulated among the students that Carlos could tell  the future. Other students with pagan backgrounds asked to have their  palms read. This went on for several weeks until a teacher discovered  what Carlos was doing and told the school principal.
After worship in the dormitory one evening, the dean of boys  asked Carlos to stay behind. “Carlos,” he said, “the school principal  wants to see you in his office.”
“When?” Carlos wanted to know.
“You should go see him right now. It’s urgent.”
Carlos headed for the principal’s office. Why does he want to see  me? What have I done? All the years I studied in San Carlos, I never got  in trouble. Why am I in trouble now?
