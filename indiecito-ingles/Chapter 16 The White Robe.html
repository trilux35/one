Chapter 16: The White Robe


All five of Carlos’s children responded to the invitation to attend the fiftieth anniversary of their aging parents. “We’re coming,”  they said. “You sacrificed so much to help us get our educa tion. We want to show our appreciation for all you have done for us.” Hundreds of people packed the Platería Adventist church on a  bright December day to celebrate the anniversary. Less than a thou sand feet from the gate where they first met at the home originally built  by Pastor Stahl, Carlos and Naomi walked down the aisle together, and  the Lake Titicaca Mission president led them in renewing vows they  had made fifty years before.
All the children and grandchildren were there. Yban and his fam ily flew in from the United States. Jaime and his family came all the  way from Mexico. Yban, the oldest living son, led out in paying trib ute to their parents. Carlos responded by telling about the benefits of  Christian education.
“My paternal grandparents,” he said, “rejected the teaching of pio neer missionary Ferdinand Stahl and wanted nothing to do with the  schools he founded. They had no education and lived in misery. My  parents were ignorant too, but God worked on their hearts, and al though they wanted nothing to do with the mission school, they sent  me to Colegio San Carlos in Puno.
“When I asked to go to Colegio Adventista del Lago Titicaca, they  said, ‘Never,’ but the Holy Spirit changed their minds. My years at  Chullunquiani, now an Adventist university, changed my life. And,  thank God, when my parents accepted Christ, their lives changed too.  All my children have been educated in Adventist schools, and I’m  amazed at what God has done for our family in just four generations.  I’ll always be grateful for pioneer missionaries like Ferdinand and  Ana Stahl, who sacrificed to help our people. I want to meet the Stahls  when Jesus comes.”

After the service in the church, a reception was held in the large  patio of Colegio Fernando Stahl—a school named in honor of its  founder. Leaders from the Mission, pastors from the area, church  members, former students, family, and friends, all rejoiced for a couple  who served God and stayed true to each other for fifty years.
“This has been an incredible day,” Carlos told his family. “It’s the  first time we’ve all been together for years. We doubted it could hap pen. And what a joy to meet our grandchildren from Mexico and the  United States.”
Yban and Jaime smiled. “It’s not over yet. We’re taking you and all the  family to Cusco and Machu Picchu. We’ll leave in the morning.” Carlos  had visited the famous Inca ruins during a teacher’s retreat, but for Naomi  this would be a first. “I’m excited,” she said. “Not only will I get to visit  Cusco again, but I can visit a famous Inca site I’ve heard about all my life.”
After several days traveling together, the family ended up back at  Carlos and Naomi’s home in Utawilaya. “It’s been a delight to honor  our parents and visit our brothers who work for the Adventist church  here in Peru,” Yban said. “And we’re happy for a dedicated sister who  works as a nurse serving Aymaras here in the Altiplano.”
“Listen, Mom and Dad,” the two older brothers continued. “The  celebration isn’t over yet. We want you to apply for Peruvian passports  and visas for Mexico and the United States. We’re sending you tickets  to come and visit us. You need to get to know your grandchildren and  see where we live.”
Carlos and Naomi gasped in unbelief. “We’ve never been out of  Peru except to walk across the Desaguadero Bridge into Bolivia.” “Don’t worry,” their sons said. “We’ll arrange everything.” The next week, Carlos and Naomi applied for passports. Later, in  Lima, they got visas. People asked them, “How can poor Indians like  you get your visas so fast? We’ve been trying for months.” “We’re Adventistas,” Carlos replied. “Our sons are doctors, and  they arranged everything.”
“What are Adventistas?” they asked.
“We’re Protestants who study the Bible and live to please Jesus.” “We need to become Adventistas too!”
Carlos thought, Maybe it’s an advantage to be a little Indian, espe cially if you are an Adventista. One thing for sure—it’s an advantage to  be a Christian. Instead of just flying to Mexico or the United States, when  Jesus comes we can travel through space all the way to heaven.

Carlos and his bride of fifty years checked in at the ticket counter,  passed through immigration, and boarded a huge jet. Holding hands  in a plane loaded with passengers, they roared off the runway at Lima’s  Jorge Chavez International Airport. Soon they were flying over Peru’s  highest mountain, Huascarán.
Six hours in the air took them from the land of the Incas to Mexico  City and the home of the Aztecs, followed by a long drive with Jaime to  his home in Oaxaca. “What I like most,” Carlos said, “is to find our son  and daughter-in-law honoring Jesus by being active in their church.”
After happy days in Jaime’s home, they traveled by bus to Laredo,  Texas, where they were met by Yban. “Welcome to the United States,”  Yban greeted his parents. “You probably found many things in Mexico  are similar to Peru. You will have many surprises here in the States.”
The family was sleeping when they arrived in Dallas at midnight.  Yban showed them their room. “You will have your own private bath room.” How can this be? Carlos wondered. Are they going outside to an  outhouse so we can use their bathroom? Weary from travel by bus and  car, they fell asleep, thankful to be with their son.
After a morning shower, Carlos called Naomi. “It’s your turn. You  will love this. I’ve never had a shower with so much hot water.” While  Naomi showered, Carlos went out to talk with his son. “Why did you  give us your bathroom? We didn’t expect you to do this for us.”
“No, no!” Yban said, “My wife and I sleep in the master bedroom.  We have our own bath. When visitors come, we want them to have  privacy, so we let the children use our bathroom.”
“Honest? I was afraid you had to use an outhouse.”
“No, Papa, even the poorest people have a bathroom. Almost  all homes are built with at least two bathrooms. Why don’t you call  mother and tell her breakfast is ready.”
Carlos went back to get Naomi. “They’re waiting for us to eat  breakfast. This is a first-class country. Can you imagine? Yban’s house  has two bathrooms.”
At breakfast, Yban led out in morning worship, just as his father  had done when he was growing up on the floating islands. Carlos and  Naomi looked at the strange food on the table. They had never seen  such an abundance of fruit and juice. There were several choices of  dry cereal, including granola, and all served with cold milk. They  didn’t complain, but it was very different from what they were used  to eating.

Later that day they discovered their son owned three cars. “What  kind of a country is this anyway?” Carlos questioned. They mar veled on Sabbath when they attended a beautiful church. Each day  in the United States brought new wonders. They were thrilled with  their grandchildren.
Back home in the highlands of Peru after their travels, Carlos sat at  the table enjoying a meal prepared by Naomi. “You know, dear,” he said.  “The United States has many wonderful things, but I like the plain, sim ple food you serve me the best. We are privileged to live in the Andes.”
“Yes, Carlos, we are blessed,” Naomi said. “I’m thankful to God  that all our children are doing well. We are getting older, and I think  it’s time for us to slow down. Let’s sell our cow. Why can’t we rest a little  and not work all the time? Perhaps we could get a sheep or two. We can  dedicate our time to God and His church.”
“You’re right. We need rest, but one thing I want to do is fix a  shower that will work for us. The hot showers we had in Texas were  wonderful.” Carlos figured out a way to put a small water tank on the  roof where water could be warmed by the sun. He rigged up some  pipes with a shower head, and the warmer water proved much better  than the icy baths they had taken all their lives.
Yban and Jaime had given them six thousand dollars to build a  better home, but the frugal parents decided, “Our simple adobe house  meets our needs.” They gave most of the money to their three children  living in Peru.
Looking forward to happy retirement days together, they attended  worship services in Utawilaya. After a fellowship dinner one Sabbath  morning, they walked to Platería to attend a youth choir program.  Carlos went home to care for the sheep in the evening, but Naomi  caught a bus to Puno to celebrate their daughter Olga’s birthday. “I’ll  be back Sunday morning,” she promised.
When she had not returned by Sunday afternoon, Carlos began  to worry. What’s happened? he questioned. We were planning to go to  Puno together on Monday.
When Naomi had not returned by late afternoon, he put the sheep  in their pen and started walking to the main road to catch a bus. He  was ready to flag down a bus when his son Daniel arrived. “Papa, we  don’t know what to do,” he said, all out of breath. “Mama’s very sick.  She started feeling bad on Saturday night. She’s been in severe pain all  day today.”

Arriving in Puno, Carlos found his wife writhing in pain. She  could hardly talk. “Naomi,” he asked. “Will you let us take you to the  Adventist clinic in Juliaca?” Groaning in pain, she nodded yes. It was  too late to go that night, so they left at dawn on Monday, accompanied  by Daniel and their daughter Olga. 
The doctors were in surgery when they arrived, and by noon,  Naomi still had not been seen by them. Carlos talked with his children.  “I think I’d better go home and leave you with Mother. I left without  getting anyone to care for our sheep. When the doctor comes, have  him do whatever is best.” He prayed for his wife all the way home.
The doctors arrived shortly after Carlos left. After making a careful  examination, they said, “You really got here too late. We must rush her  into surgery because we think she has a ruptured appendix. We’ll do  our best to repair the damage and clean out all the infection.”
Naomi woke up late that afternoon. “Where am I?” she wanted to  know. “Where’s Carlos? Please tell him I want to see him.” Daniel left for Utawilaya to find his father. “Papa,” he said on his  arrival. “Mama had surgery for a ruptured appendix. She wants to see  you. The doctors are giving her heavy doses of antibiotics to fight in fection.” By the time Carlos found someone to watch the sheep, it was  too late to go to the hospital that night.
He arrived at Naomi’s bedside before dawn on Tuesday morning.  She opened her eyes when he took her hand, and she looked directly at  him. “God has blessed us with a wonderful life together. I love you and  the children and Jesus.” She paused. “Carlos, I’m going to rest now.”  Her eyes closed.
Wednesday, Thursday, Friday—Carlos and his children prayed.  The doctors did everything they could, but there was no improvement.  Naomi never talked again. She was not getting better. Carlos was by  her side when she took her last breath on Sabbath, June 10, 2005.
Tears flooded his eyes as he thought, I’ll never forget how she looked  at me and said, “I’m going to rest now.” She knew she was going to die,  and I know she was ready.
Carlos recalled the words of Jesus when he spoke to His disciples,  saying, “Our friend Lazarus sleeps, but I go that I may wake him up”  (John 11:11). It’s true that death is like a sleep. I just can’t wait until Jesus  comes to wake her up.
Jaime flew in from Mexico. Yban came from the United States.  Efraín, Olga, and Daniel had been at the hospital most of the week. At

the funeral service, held in the chapel at Chullunquiani on Monday,  Pastor Rubén Jaimes presented a message of hope. Jaime paid a tribute  to his mother.
When the service finished, Naomi’s four living sons acted as pall bearers, taking their mother to a vehicle waiting to take her to the cem etery. The Velasquez family, accompanied by mission workers, friends,  and former students, walked up the steep, rocky path to the cemetery  high on the hill behind the Adventist university.
Pastor Jaimes concluded the graveside service with words of Jesus.  “I am the resurrection and the life. He who believes in Me, though he  may die, he shall live” (John 11:25). Carlos struggled to hold back tears  as they lowered Naomi’s casket into the grave. He remembered words  of Scripture: “The days of our lives are seventy years” (Psalm 90:10).  Thoughts of the past flooded his mind.
God has been gracious to give Naomi seventy years, and I will al ways be thankful we had fifty of those years together. She put up with so  much during our first year of marriage when the school failed to pay my  salary. Our years in Cusco were a struggle, but she never complained.  Few women would have sacrificed as she did to serve the Uros on the  floating islands of Lake Titicaca.
All five living children accompanied their father to his home, but  too soon they had to return to their work. Carlos bought gladiolus  bulbs and planted them in memory of his wife. He cried every day for  a month. All alone, he cared for the sheep, kept things up around his  home, went to church, but grieved. How can I live without her? Why did  this have to happen? 
The tomb of Chief Camacho, the Aymara Indian who befriended  Pastor Stahl, is located within walking distance of Carlos’s home.  Walking by one day, Carlos had an idea. I’m going to build a burial  site for Naomi and me right here on our property. He finished the  tombs and got ready to move Naomi’s body from the university cem
etery. “Don’t be foolish, Dad,” his children said and talked him out of  it. “Just let Mother rest where she is. It won’t make any difference at  the resurrection.”
Yban knew it must be difficult for Carlos to live alone. He sent a  message saying, “Papa, my family and I want you to come and live with  us in the United States. I’ll send you a ticket.”
Arriving at his son’s home in Dallas, Carlos stopped grieving. God  is so good, he thought. Whoever dreamed Yban would take care of me

like this. What a joy to live in a home with two bathrooms and lots of  hot water.
One day, Yban announced, “Papa, we are going to take you to  Michigan. You can see Andrews University. We’ll visit Battle Creek,  where the Adventist Church was first organized. You can see the old  Battle Creek Sanitarium where Ferdinand Stahl worked as a nurse  while his wife, Ana, studied nursing.”
“Where did Elder Stahl get his nursing education?” Carlos asked. “Oh, he took nursing at Madison, Wisconsin,” Yban replied.  Visiting historic places made Carlos happy. Whoever thought I’d 
get to visit a city where the Stahls lived? What a blessing they were to all  of us who live in Peru. The sacrifices they made to establish God’s work in  the Andes and Amazon will always be appreciated.
At the Battle Creek cemetery, Carlos visited graves of Adventist  pioneers he had often read about. Standing by the grave of Ellen White,  he started thinking about all the Ellen G. White books he’d read.
The book Education gave me a vision of Christian education that  guided me during all the years of my teaching ministry. I read The  Adventist Home before marrying Naomi, and it inspired me to always  make Christ the center of our home. We did our best to follow princi
ples outlined in the book, Child Guidance. I think that is one reason all  our children completed their education and are faithful to Jesus and His  church today.
Standing where many church pioneers are buried, Carlos silently  prayed. “Dear Father, thank you for faithful pioneers who determined  to take the message of a soon-coming Savior to the entire world. Thank  You for books like the Conflict of the Ages series that have helped me  understand the teachings of the Bible. Thank you for dedicated people  like Ferdinand and Ana Stahl, who, after spending time at the Battle  Creek Sanitarium, were inspired to serve as missionaries in the Andes  and Amazon.”
Back in Texas, Carlos kept thinking about the wonderful places  he’d visited. Whoever dreamed I would have such an opportunity? What  he didn’t know was that while he was staying in Texas, a group led by  the Slikker family from Michigan were building a floating church for  the Uro Indians at Lake Titicaca’s floating islands.
This man who had lived all his life in the high Andes, where the  temperature seldom gets above fifty degrees, struggled with the Texas  heat. He talked with his son. “Maybe it’s because I’m really just a little

Indian, but I want to go home. I can’t spend my life in an air-condi tioned room, and it’s too hot outside.”
He continued, “I love the hot water. You’ve given me the best food,  but I’m not used to eating like Americans. I appreciate all you’ve done  for me, but I think it’s best for me to live out my life where I grew up.”
After Carlos arrived back at his home in Peru, I had the privilege  of joining the Mission president to take Carlos to see the new floating  church built by Maranatha Volunteers International. Tears welled up in  his eyes when he saw the floating church for the first time. Astonished,  he exclaimed, “God answers prayer. The Holy Spirit has touched many  hearts to make this possible. Give God the glory!”
He marveled. “I’ve prayed many years for this. The church is beau tiful. Praise God the Uros have a place to sing, worship, pray, and study  God’s Word. Not only is it a blessing to the Uros, but it’s a witness to the  hundreds of tourists who visit the floating islands every day.”
Carlos says, “I thank God for giving me the privilege of starting a  floating school for the Uros. I’m thankful for others who followed me,  making it possible for the floating school to continue today. I’m thank ful to be part of a church that believes in taking the gospel ‘to every  nation, tribe, tongue, and people’ [Revelation 14:6].”
As I write this book, Carlos is in his eighties and lives alone. But,  he says, “I’m not alone. God is with me. I get exercise taking care of  my sheep. I study, I pray, and I help the church as much as I can. I  thank God that He will let me live long enough to take part in the one
hundredth anniversary of the arrival of Pastor and Mrs. Ferdinand  Stahl in 1911.”
God took a little Indian and used him to transform the lives of  hundreds of Uros. Some of his students became teachers. Others are  church workers. One went as a missionary to Bolivia.
Outside his home in Utawilaya, Carlos painted a rainbow on the  side of the place where he plans to be buried. Below the rainbow are  these words: “CHRIST IS COMING—BE READY.”
Jesus won’t ask Carlos to wear a necktie. The Savior has some thing far better. He offers His saints a “white robe,” the robe of Christ’s  righteousness. Carlos can walk through heaven’s gate wearing a white  robe that has been washed in the blood of the Lamb.
