Chapter 3: Song of Songs


The Spanish conquerors, the conquistadors, brought the Catholic religion to the Inca Empire in the Andes. Quechua  and Aymara Indians like Carlos’s parents assimilated into the  new religion while keeping their own. This led to a strange mixture  of Christianity and paganism. Carlos’s parents, like their neighbors,  venerated the saints, had their children baptized as infants, took part  in the church’s feast days, and attended Mass. At the same time, they  worshiped the sun, held to the superstitions of their fathers, prayed to  the spirits, and practiced witchcraft.
While studying in Puno, Carlos learned to sing to the Virgin  Mary, recite the Rosary, repeat the Lord’s Prayer, and study from a cat echism. He became puzzled when a biology teacher taught that men  and women descended from monkeys over long periods of time. This  is garbage, he thought. Inca legends make much more sense. Inti, the sun  god, and Mama Quilla, the moon god, brought their children, Manco  Capac and Mama Ocllo, out of Lake Titicaca to form the Incas.
After school one day, a group of older boys approached Carlos.  “We’re going to serenade girls tonight. Why don’t you come along?”  Surprised to be invited since he was only fifteen, Carlos thought, This  is great. I see pretty girls in my class, but I’m too scared to talk to them.
“Sure, I’ll go with you,” he said. The boys waited until it was time  for the girls to be in bed before they started out. Standing next to a  house, they sang old-fashioned love songs. After they sang for a while,  girls on the second floor opened a window and stuck their heads out,  smiling and waving. This is exciting, Carlos thought. I wish we knew  more songs.
A few days later, a priest walked into a religion class and held up  a book Carlos had never seen before. “This is a Bible,” the priest an nounced as he turned the pages. “It’s made up of the Old and New  Testaments. There’s the Law and the Prophets. These record the history

of God’s people from the beginning of the world until just before Jesus  was born in Bethlehem.”
Boring, Carlos thought, but the priest kept right on talking.  “What we call the Old Testament also contains the Psalms, Proverbs,  Ecclesiastes, and the Song of Solomon. Listen to this.” The priest paused  and began to read. “The song of songs, which is Solomon’s” (Song of  Solomon 1:1).
Song of Songs! It hit Carlos like a lightning strike. Suddenly he be came interested in the Bible. I’ve got to get this book. With the “Song of  Songs” we could have a lot more fun serenading girls.
He approached the priest at the close of class. “I want to buy a  Bible,” he said.
The priest smiled. “I’m sorry, but we don’t have Bibles for sale.”  Disappointed, Carlos walked back toward his room on Calle de la  Libertad. He whistled when two attractive girls crossed the street.  He knew their names but kept his distance and didn’t talk to them.  Occasionally he waved at girls at soccer games. He liked them all but  didn’t have any special girl friends.
Song of Songs! He thought about it walking down the street. Back  in his room, he couldn’t get it out of his head. It whirled around in his  brain while he worked. He still thought about it while wrapped in wool  blankets lying on the dirt floor, trying to go to sleep. 
Song of Songs! It flashed into his mind when he got dressed the  next morning. He dreamed about it at school. Every day—the same  thought. I’ve got to get a Bible. I want to learn the Song of Songs. He  went to a bookstore, but they had no Bibles. He asked friends where  he could buy a Bible, but no one knew. It was becoming an obsession.
The school year ended, and Carlos went home for summer vaca tion. His desire to purchase a Bible weighed on his mind, but he did  not mention it to his parents. Rather than waste time going to Sunday  markets, he preferred to stay on the farm and watch sheep. “Mercados are boring,” he always said.
Where can I get a Bible? he still wondered often. I need the Song of  Songs—I’ve got to find a Bible. Early one Sunday, he walked across the  pampa toward Platería. Why am I doing this? he thought. I hate mar kets. Something seemed to be pushing him.
Feeling lonely, since all his friends were in Puno, Carlos walked  around the Platería market. Merchandise of every kind was on display,  but nothing interested him. A man selling books caught his attention.

He walked closer. His eyes were drawn to a black book with gold let ters. He stood and stared. Holy Bible! He wanted to shout, Is this the  book the priest told us about?
He approached the vendor. “Sir, does this book contain the Song  of Songs?”
“Yes, it has sixty-six different books, including the Song of Songs.” Carlos asked, “How much does it cost?”
“It’s a bargain. You can buy it for sixty centavos.”
Carlos searched through his pockets. Amazing, he mused. I’ve got  exactly sixty centavos. I can’t wait to get back to school and serenade the  girls. How will they react when we sing the Song of Songs?
Thoughts of smiling girls waving from second-floor windows  raced through Carlos’s mind. I don’t want anyone seeing me buy a Bible.  I’ll just stand here until the people move on by.
At last, no one was around except the book seller. Carlos handed  the man sixty centavos, took the Bible, and hid it under his clothes. He  walked away from the plaza and out into the pampa. Finally, with no  one near, he stopped, pulled out the book and started looking for the  Song of Songs, as the Song of Solomon is called in most Spanish versions of the Bible.
What’s wrong? The vendor said this book has the Song of Songs, but  I can’t find it. Carlos was getting desperate until he found the index.  Whew, it’s here. It does have the Song of Songs.
Quickly he looked up the page number and began to read. What? I  don’t see any song. He scanned through all eight chapters. These are not  songs to serenade girls! Big mistake. I wasted my money.
During the summer at home, Carlos became curious and began  looking at the Bible when no one was around. He read how God made  the world in six days. That makes sense, he thought. My biology teacher  needs to read this.
Wanting to know more about the Bible, Carlos kept reading, but he  didn’t understand much of it. I need someone to teach me, he thought.  It’s time to register for school. There’s a school in Chullunquiani where  they teach Bible. That’s where I should go.
For no particular reason, Carlos went to visit a maternal cousin  named Yupanqui, whose father was an Adventista. Alone at home, the  cousin invited Carlos to come in. The boys chatted about their sum mer work on their parents’ farms. Looking around the room, Carlos  noticed some magazines. The Spanish title of one, La Luz de los Andes

(Light of the Andes), grabbed his attention. Carlos picked it up and  began browsing.
“Where did you get this?” he asked.
“Oh, it’s published by a school at Chullunquiani.”
“I see it advertises Colegio del Lago Titicaca. Where is this school?” “It’s more than fifty miles away, on the other side of Juliaca.” Carlos looked at his cousin and said, “I’m going to Chullunquiani.  I want to attend this school.”
“You go to Chullunquiani?” His cousin laughed. “What will you do  there? It’s not like Colegio San Carlos. The people there are Adventista Christians. They study the Bible. They pray and sing. Do you know  how to sing hymns?”
A moment later, Yupanqui added, “My father wants me to go to a  Christian school, but I’m not ready for that.”
“You’re lucky,” Carlos said. “My parents want nothing to do with  Adventista schools.” He thought for a minute. “I’ve made up my mind.  I’m going to Chullunquiani.”
Carlos forgot all about the Song of Songs and began dreaming  about Chullunquiani. Walking along the stony trail back to his home  in Ccota, his mind focused on Chullunquiani. At home that evening— Chullunquiani. When he got up in the morning—Chullunquiani. All  day long he kept thinking, I’ve got to go to Chullunquiani.
It was early March and time for Carlos to register for classes. His  parents’ home had been enlarged, and they had their own little bed room. Because of the chilling cold in the Peruvian highlands, Aymaras  went to bed early. Carlos walked into the bedroom. “Papa,” he said. “I  want to study at Chullunquiani.”
“Never!” his father growled. “You aren’t going to Chullunquiani!  Those people are Adventistas. They sing a lot at that school. Do I sing?  Does your mother sing? Do you know how to sing? It’s no place for you.”
Chullunquiani! My parents won’t let me go, Carlos thought. With eyes fixed on his parents, the firstborn son spoke po litely. “Thank you, Papa. Thank you, Mama. Since I’m not going to  Chullunquiani, I’ve decided not to study anymore. I’m grown up, so  I’ll find a job and work. I finished primary school and have a year of  secondary. I’m going to Arequipa to get a job and earn money. I’ll leave  at midnight tonight.”
His stunned parents listened in silence. Why is Carlos so rash? his  father thought. Troubled thoughts filled the mind of Emilio Velasquez.

I wanted my son to be a witch doctor. We sacrificed to send him to Puno  to get an education, and now he wants to go to Chullunquiani. My par ents rightly refused the message of Stahl. Why would my firstborn want  to get mixed up with that crazy religion?
The angry father sat up and shouted, “Get out of here! Go to bed!” Respectfully, Carlos stepped closer to his parents and repeated  words of appreciation. “Thank you, Papa. Thank you, Mama. You’ve  helped me a lot. I know how to take care of myself. I’ll be OK. I’m going  to Arequipa. You won’t need to worry about me.”
Carlos turned and walked back to his own bed. It’s going to be dif ficult, he realized. There are no cars or buses, and I can’t afford the train.  Oh well, a chullo and poncho will keep me warm. I’ll walk across the  Andes and get to Arequipa with no problem.
As he thought about the plans he had announced to his parents,  he began to wonder, What have I done now? I told them I’m leaving.  Should I go or should I stay? To start out walking to Arequipa tonight  might not be too smart.
Carlos’s active mind searched for a better solution. I know, he thought. I’ll go to Puno, work a month, and buy a train ticket to  Arequipa. While looking for a better job, I can sell water. Puno has two  public fountains. The best one is behind the cathedral. Hardly anyone has  running water, so I’ll sell buckets of water.
Carlos jumped up and put on his poncho. He got a little food to gether. Now I’m ready. I’ll lie down and rest until midnight. Since I know  Puno well, this will work out fine. And when I get to Arequipa, I can start  a new life.
